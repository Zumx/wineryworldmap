Germany makes some of the world's most age-worthy white wine, grown on slopes
so steep they are worked by hand. These ten estates are reference points for
Riesling and beyond.

## 1. Egon Müller — Scharzhof, Saar

The most famous Riesling estate in the world; its Scharzhofberger Auslese and
TBA fetch legendary prices.

## 2. Joh. Jos. Prüm, Mosel

The benchmark Mosel estate — Wehlener Sonnenuhr Rieslings built to age for
decades.

## 3. Dr. Loosen, Mosel

A global ambassador for Mosel Riesling with grand-cru sites and a welcoming
visitor program.

## 4. Weingut Robert Weil, Rheingau

The Rheingau's modern standard-bearer, immaculate Kiedrich Riesling across
every sweetness level.

## 5. Schloss Johannisberg, Rheingau

The historic estate where late-harvest Riesling was supposedly discovered — a
hilltop landmark.

## 6. Maximin Grünhaus, Ruwer

A single great monopole hillside producing crystalline Ruwer Riesling.

## 7. Keller, Rheinhessen

A cult dry-Riesling estate whose "G-Max" is one of the most sought-after
wines in the world.

## 8. Künstler, Rheingau

Powerful, precise dry Rieslings from top Hochheim sites.

## 9. Fürst, Franconia

Germany's finest Pinot Noir and Silvaner producer, proving German reds belong
in the conversation.

## 10. Heymann-Löwenstein, Terraced Mosel

Dramatic terraced-slope Rieslings expressing slate terroir at its most
mineral.

## How German winery visits work

Many estates welcome visitors for tastings (*Weinprobe*) by appointment;
cellar-door culture is strong in wine villages along the Mosel and in the
Rheingau. Cult estates (Egon Müller, Keller) are not casual drop-ins — book
well ahead or visit via a specialist. Wine festivals (late summer–autumn) are
the easy, open way in.

## Plan around the rivers

German wine routes follow rivers: the Mosel's hairpin slopes, the Rheingau's
Rhine terrace, the Nahe. Pick one river valley and base in a wine village —
distances are short and many estates are walkable or a short drive apart.

## Taste responsibly

German Rieslings are often lower in alcohol than international wine, but
multi-estate days still add up — use the spittoon, drink water, and never
drive between tastings. The Mosel's steep, winding roads make a driver or
wine-route shuttle essential.
