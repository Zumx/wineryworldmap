Nebbiolo is the most demanding red of all — long-ripening,
tannin-heavy, and obstinate about where it will grow. Outside the
Langhe hills around Alba, only a few sites (Valtellina, Carema, the
Roero) can coax greatness from it. Inside the Langhe, the modern-
traditional split shapes producer style.

## Traditional Barolo

Long maceration, large old wood, no new barriques. Giacomo Conterno
(Monforte), Giuseppe Mascarello (Castiglione Falletto), Bartolo
Mascarello (Barolo), Bruno Giacosa (Neive, also a great Barbaresco
maker), and Cavallotto (Castiglione Falletto) are touchstones.

## Modernist Barolo

Shorter macerations, smaller oak, more international styling.
Elio Altare (La Morra), Paolo Scavino (Castiglione Falletto), and
Roberto Voerzio (La Morra) led the movement. Most have moderated
toward the middle ground since.

## Middle ground

The largest cohort today — fastidious viticulture, classical
methods, occasional small oak. Vietti (Castiglione Falletto), Aldo
Conterno (Monforte), Vajra (Vergne above Barolo), Brovia
(Castiglione Falletto), Massolino (Serralunga), and Cordero di
Montezemolo (La Morra).

## Barbaresco

Slightly more accessible, earlier-drinking Nebbiolo. Gaja (the
international name), Bruno Giacosa (red label Riservas), Produttori
del Barbaresco (the great cooperative), Cantina del Pino, Castello
di Neive, and Sottimano are reference producers.

## Roero

Across the Tanaro from the Langhe. Matteo Correggia and Malvirà are
the reference producers; the same Nebbiolo variety produces a
slightly lighter, more red-fruited wine on these sands.

## Valtellina (Lombardy)

A small Alpine Nebbiolo zone where the grape is called Chiavennasca.
ARPEPE, Sandro Fay, and Nino Negri are reference producers; the
wines are leaner and higher-acid than Langhe Nebbiolo.

## Carema

A tiny Piedmont alpine appellation with pergola-trained Nebbiolo on
terraces. Produttori di Carema and Ferrando are the two reference
estates; tiny production, profound longevity.
