Natural wine — a loose label for wines made with native yeasts,
without commercial additions, often with minimal or zero sulphites
— has reshaped wine lists in Paris, Tokyo, and Brooklyn since the
early 2000s. The movement has roots in several specific
geographies; these are the estates that anchor it.

## Beaujolais (the original natural wine movement)

The cradle. Jules Chauvet's research in the 1960s and 1970s inspired
Marcel Lapierre, Jean Foillard, Guy Breton, and Jean-Paul Thévenet —
the "Gang of Four" — to make sulphite-free Gamay from the granite
hills of Morgon. Their descendants and disciples now define the
appellation.

## Jura

Pierre Overnoy, Jean-François Ganevat, and Domaine de la Tournelle
in Pupillin/Arbois lead the Jura natural movement; oxidative
savagnin and *ouillé* (topped-up) chardonnay are the signature
wines.

## Loire

Olivier Cousin (Anjou), Mark Angeli (Coteaux du Layon), Catherine
& Pierre Breton (Chinon), Thierry Puzelat (Cheverny), and Nicolas
Joly (Savennières) span the Loire natural movement from
biodynamic Chenin to no-sulphite Cabernet Franc.

## Alsace

Domaine Ostertag, Pierre Frick, and Christian Binner are leading
natural-leaning Alsace estates; some skin-fermented Pinot Gris
expressions cross into orange wine.

## Italy: Etna and beyond

The volcanic Etna slopes — Frank Cornelissen, Vino di Anna, and I
Vigneri (Salvo Foti) — are major natural producers. Radikon and
Gravner in Friuli pioneered orange wine; La Stoppa in Emilia
champions natural Barbera and Trebbiano.

## Georgia

The historical natural wine — Qvevri-fermented wines from clay
amphorae buried in the ground. Pheasant's Tears, Iago Bitarishvili,
Our Wine (Soliko Tsaishvili), and Doremi are reference natural
estates.

## Australia: the Adelaide Hills

Australia's most active natural wine scene. Lucy Margaux (Anton van
Klopper), Jauma (James Erskine), Ochota Barrels, and Commune of
Buttons are reference natural producers.

## US: California

Donkey & Goat, Scholium Project, and Broc Cellars in California;
Day Wines and Statera in Oregon — the New World natural movement
leaning Burgundian rather than oxidative.
