Wine in Japan is older than most outsiders realise. Christian
missionaries brought viticulture to Kyushu in the 16th century;
modern commercial production began with two young men sent to
France by the Meiji government in 1877. Yamanashi prefecture, in
the Kofu basin below Mount Fuji, has been the heartland ever since,
and Hokkaido has lately emerged as a cool-climate alternative.
Japanese wine — particularly from the native Koshu grape — has
its own clear identity: delicate, low-alcohol, restrained, often
made to pair with washoku cuisine.

## Grace Wine (Chuo Budoshu)

The benchmark Koshu producer. Three generations of the Misawa family
have refined the Grace style; Ayana Misawa, the current winemaker,
trained in Bordeaux and Burgundy. Cuvée Misawa Akeno Koshu is the
estate's flagship and was among the first Japanese wines to win
international critical recognition.

## Château Mercian

The largest serious wine producer in Japan, founded in 1877 from
the original government-sponsored Meiji-era venture. Mercian's
Hosaka Vineyard Chardonnay and Mariko Vineyard Omnis (Bordeaux
blend) are pioneering single-vineyard Japanese wines; the Katsunuma
Winery sits at the heart of Yamanashi wine country.

## Suntory Tomi no Oka Winery

Suntory's wine estate in the Tomi hills of Yamanashi. The Tomi
Rouge (a Cabernet/Merlot blend) and Tomi Koshu show Suntory's
characteristic technical polish; the winery itself has been a
focus of Japanese wine tourism since the 1970s.

## Coco Farm & Winery

In Tochigi prefecture, north of Tokyo. The estate began as a farm
school for adults with intellectual disabilities and has grown
into one of Japan's most respected and idiosyncratic small wineries.
The Norubeshi field-blend and varietal Koshu and Tannat bottlings
are highly regarded.

## Domaine Takahiko

In Hokkaido's Yoichi region, Takahiko Soga has become the most
discussed natural and Pinot Noir-focused winemaker in Japan. The
Nana-Tsu-Mori Pinot Noir bottlings are among the most ambitious
red wines made in the country and are extremely allocated.

## Domaine de Montille (Hokkaido)

The Burgundian house of Domaine de Montille planted Pinot Noir and
Chardonnay in Hakodate, Hokkaido in 2018, in partnership with
local investors. Early-release wines have demonstrated that
Hokkaido can produce a serious, Burgundian-style Pinot Noir.

## Manns Wines (Kikkoman)

Owned by the soy-sauce giant Kikkoman. Manns has substantial
holdings in Yamanashi and Nagano. The Solaris Shinano Riesling
series from Nagano has demonstrated that Japan can produce
ageworthy aromatic whites; the Manns Komoro Chardonnay is a
benchmark.

## L'Orient Wine (Shirayuri Winery)

Family estate in Katsunuma since 1938. The L'Orient Koshu sur Lie
and L'Orient Muscat Bailey A series have established the estate
as a reliable mid-range Yamanashi reference; the Katsunuma village
location makes it an essential visit on any Yamanashi wine tour.

## 10R Winery / 10R Wineworks

Bruce Gutlove's Hokkaido winery — Gutlove is one of the most
respected outside winemakers in Japan, having trained dozens of
the country's younger winemakers. The 10R bottlings are made by
multiple producers using the shared facility; quality varies but
the best are exceptional.

## Takeda Winery (Yamagata)

Yamagata-prefecture estate run by the Kishimura family, with a
strong Cabernet Sauvignon programme — among the few Japanese
estates to take Bordeaux varietals seriously. Domaine Takeda
Cuvée Yoshiko is the flagship.

## Visiting Japanese Wine Country

Yamanashi's Katsunuma district is an hour by train from Shinjuku
in Tokyo — many wineries open for casual tastings, particularly
during the autumn harvest season. Hokkaido's Yoichi region
clusters Pinot Noir producers north-west of Sapporo. Find every
