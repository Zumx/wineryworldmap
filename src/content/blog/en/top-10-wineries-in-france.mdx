No country has shaped the language of fine wine like France. Its
appellation system, its grape varieties, and its concept of *terroir*
are the templates the rest of the world borrows. Choosing ten
producers from a country with thousands of great estates is an act
of provocation — but the names below, drawn from Bordeaux, Burgundy,
Champagne and the Rhône, are the reference points against which the
world measures itself.

## 1. Domaine de la Romanée-Conti (Burgundy)

The most coveted wine on Earth. The Domaine, in Vosne-Romanée, owns
the entire grand-cru monopoles of Romanée-Conti and La Tâche and
holds parcels in Richebourg, Romanée-Saint-Vivant, Échézeaux and
Montrachet. Biodynamic, microscopic in yield, and almost
unobtainable, its Pinot Noir is the benchmark for the variety.

## 2. Château Lafite Rothschild (Bordeaux)

A first growth of the 1855 classification, in Pauillac on the Left
Bank. Lafite's Cabernet Sauvignon–dominant blend is the most
ethereal and perfumed of the first growths, prized in Asia above all
others. Owned by the Rothschild family since 1868.

## 3. Château Margaux (Bordeaux)

The first growth of the Margaux appellation, famed for fragrance and
silk-textured tannin. The neoclassical château is itself a monument;
the wine, a Cabernet-led blend, is among the most consistent of all
Bordeaux at the very top level.

## 4. Petrus (Bordeaux)

The legend of Pomerol, made from almost pure Merlot on a unique
button of blue clay. Unclassified — the Right Bank has no 1855
ranking — yet priced among the most expensive wines in the world. A
tiny 11.5-hectare vineyard producing wine of extraordinary density.

## 5. Domaine Leflaive (Burgundy)

The reference for white Burgundy, based in Puligny-Montrachet.
Biodynamic since the 1990s under the late Anne-Claude Leflaive, the
domaine works grand-cru parcels in Montrachet, Chevalier-Montrachet
and Bâtard-Montrachet — the summit of Chardonnay.

## 6. Krug (Champagne)

Founded in 1843 in Reims, Krug is the house of prestige Champagne.
The multi-vintage Grande Cuvée blends well over a hundred reserve
wines across many years; Clos du Mesnil and Clos d'Ambonnay are
single-vineyard expressions of breathtaking rarity.

## 7. Château d'Yquem (Bordeaux)

The only Premier Cru Supérieur of Sauternes, and the greatest
botrytised sweet wine in the world. Yquem harvests Sémillon and
Sauvignon Blanc grape by grape across multiple passes, and in poor
years declassifies the entire crop rather than release a flawed
vintage.

## 8. E. Guigal (Rhône)

The dominant house of the northern Rhône, based in Ampuis. Guigal's
single-vineyard Côte-Rôtie "La-Las" — La Mouline, La Landonne, La
Turque — are cult Syrahs, and the house also produces large volumes
of reliable Côtes du Rhône that funded the empire.

## 9. Domaine Armand Rousseau (Burgundy)

The benchmark of Gevrey-Chambertin, with holdings in the grands crus
Chambertin and Chambertin-Clos de Bèze. Rousseau's red Burgundies
are structured, age-worthy and increasingly unobtainable — the
classical expression of the Côte de Nuits.

## 10. Château Cheval Blanc (Bordeaux)

A Premier Grand Cru Classé "A" of Saint-Émilion, unusual for its
high proportion of Cabernet Franc alongside Merlot. The 1947 vintage
is among the most celebrated wines ever made. A striking modern
cellar by Christian de Portzamparc opened in 2011.

## Understanding French appellations

The Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée (AOC) system, formalised in
1935, ties a wine to a defined place and a set of rules — permitted
grapes, yields, minimum ripeness. Bordeaux ranks châteaux (the 1855
classification, the Saint-Émilion and Graves classifications);
Burgundy ranks vineyards (village, premier cru, grand cru); Champagne
ranks villages on the échelle des crus. Reading a French label means
reading the place first and the producer second.

## Visiting French wine country

Bordeaux is the easiest to tour, with a compact city and the
châteaux of the Médoc, Saint-Émilion and Sauternes within an hour's
drive. Burgundy rewards the patient: the Côte d'Or is a thin ribbon
of vines south of Dijon where the top domaines are small and visits
require connections. Champagne centres on Reims and Épernay; the
northern Rhône follows the river south from Vienne. Reservations are
essential almost everywhere at this level.
