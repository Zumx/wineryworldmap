Château Margaux is one of five wines classified as a Premier Grand
Cru Classé in the 1855 ranking of Bordeaux. Its neo-Palladian
château, built 1810-1815, is the most photographed wine building in
the world. Behind the symmetry lie 87 hectares of Garonne gravels and
a winemaking lineage that runs back to the 17th century.

## The estate

The Mentzelopoulos family acquired Margaux in 1977 and brought it
back from a low point. Corinne Mentzelopoulos remains the
proprietor; her director Philippe Bascaules and consulting oenologist
Eric Boissenot run the day-to-day. The vineyard is 75% Cabernet
Sauvignon, 20% Merlot, with the balance Cabernet Franc, Petit
Verdot, and a tiny amount of Sauvignon Blanc and Sémillon for the
white.

## The terroir

The estate sits on the deepest gravel beds in the Médoc — Garonne
gravels left by ancient river courses, with limestone and clay
underneath. These deep, well-drained gravels are what give Margaux
its perfumed, ethereal character; the same vineyards in heavier
soils would make sturdier, less aromatic wines.

## The grand vin

The grand vin is famously perfumed and silky-textured — sometimes
called the Lafite to Latour's Mouton. Vintages such as 1900, 1928,
1953, 1961, 1982, 1990, 1996, 2000, 2009, 2015, and 2016 are
benchmarks. The estate has a reputation for making one of the most
graceful Bordeaux even in difficult years.

## The second wine: Pavillon Rouge

Pavillon Rouge du Château Margaux has been released since 1908,
making it the oldest declared second wine in Bordeaux. It uses
parcels deemed not quite worthy of the grand vin and offers a
genuine taste of estate character at a fraction of the price.

## The white: Pavillon Blanc

Pavillon Blanc du Château Margaux is one of the rare white wines
produced on a Médoc classed-growth property. 100% Sauvignon Blanc,
aged in oak, with a reputation for unusual longevity for white
Bordeaux.

## The Norman Foster cellar

In 2015 the estate completed a new cellar designed by Lord Norman
Foster — a contemporary glass-and-steel addition that doubled the
estate's working space without altering the historic château. It
hosts the new vinification cellar and a research laboratory.

## Visiting

Visits to Château Margaux are extremely restricted, by appointment
only, and primarily for the trade. The neo-Palladian château can be
seen from the public road south of the village of Margaux, and the
nearby Cantenac village has tasting rooms that carry the grand vin
at the appropriate price.
