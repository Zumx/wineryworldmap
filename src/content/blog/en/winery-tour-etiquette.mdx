Visiting a winery is not like visiting a shop. The estate is someone's farm, workplace, and frequently home. The people pouring wine are often the winemakers or winemaking family. The wine being tasted is the product of a year's work in the vineyard and the cellar. Understanding the protocols — what to book in advance, what the tasting fee is actually for, when to spit, what to do in the shop — is the difference between being a good guest who is welcomed back and a visitor who leaves an impression of ignorance or entitlement.

## Book Ahead: The Access Problem

The first thing to understand is that many of the world's best wineries are not accessible without a prior appointment, and some are effectively impossible to visit at all. In Burgundy, the greatest domaines — Romanée-Conti, Leroy, Leflaive, Rousseau, Mugnier, Coche-Dury — do not receive casual visitors. Appointments, when available at all, come through wine merchants who hold long-standing relationships with the estates, or through professional trade connections. Visiting these estates as a consumer requires patience, connections, and generally the willingness to be a buyer over many years. There is no shortcut. The expectation that a famous estate should be open to any visitor is a misunderstanding of how fine wine estates operate.

In Napa Valley, the problem is similar but different in form. The most sought-after cult producers — Screaming Eagle, Harlan Estate, Scarecrow, Bryant Family — do not offer public tours or tastings at all. Access is exclusively through mailing lists, and membership on those lists typically requires purchasing wine for years before an allocation is offered. Even Opus One and Robert Mondavi require advance booking for their premium tasting experiences.

For estates that do accept bookings — which includes the majority of quality producers globally — booking a week or more in advance is standard. During harvest (September-October in Europe, August-October in the Northern Hemisphere), many estates close tours entirely to focus on winemaking. Book early, confirm the week before your visit, and understand that a small estate where the winemaker is the only tasting staff cannot accommodate walk-ins without notice.

## Tasting Fees: What You Are Paying For

The era of complimentary tastings has largely ended at quality estates, particularly in high-demand regions. Napa Valley tasting fees have risen sharply in the past decade: basic tastings at mid-range estates run $25-50 per person; reserve and library-wine experiences at prestige estates typically cost $75-150 or more, with some charging $200-400 for fully guided, food-paired experiences. These are not extraction exercises — they reflect the real cost of hospitality staff, small-production wines poured, and the physical infrastructure of a visitor-friendly winery.

In Europe, the structure varies considerably by region. In Burgundy, many village-level estates offer tastings without charge, though a purchase is implicitly expected. In Champagne, the grandes maisons charge for cellar tours and tastings but price them accessibly as part of a deliberate tourist economy. In Tuscany, a tasting fee of 15-30 euros is standard at quality estates; in Germany and Austria, smaller family estates often taste without charge. Understanding the local norm prevents awkward surprises.

When a fee is charged, it is standard practice in many regions (particularly the US) for the fee to be waived with a purchase — though this is not universal and should not be assumed. In Europe, the fee stands whether you buy or not.

## Spitting Is Acceptable. Use It.

At any tasting involving more than a glass or two of wine, spitting is not merely acceptable but the mark of a serious visitor. Wineries provide spittoons (dump buckets) for exactly this purpose. A visitor who tastes six wines and consumes them all is spending the second half of the visit unable to assess the wine accurately; a visitor who spits confidently and asks good questions signals that they are genuinely interested in the wine rather than the free alcohol.

In a winery tasting room, it is never necessary to apologise for spitting. At formal seated tastings or wine dinners, the context may differ — if a tasting is structured as a paired meal, spitting is less expected. But at any stand-up winery visit, spitting every wine or every other wine is not only acceptable but welcomed by hosts who want serious engagement with their wines.

## Private vs Public Tours

The distinction between a public (walk-in or standard-booked) tour and a private tour matters significantly at many estates. A public tour may cover the standard visitor path — barrel hall, winery explanation, three to five wines poured in a tasting room. A private tour, booked specifically and at higher cost, typically provides access to parts of the estate not on the standard path: old vintage cellars, library wines, the vineyard itself with the winemaker, and wines not available in the standard pour.

At estates where private tours are available, they represent the most genuinely educational experience. The difference between tasting a wine in a tasting room and tasting it standing in the vineyard block where the grapes were grown, with the person who made it, is the difference between reading about a place and visiting it. Private tours in Barolo, Burgundy, Rioja, and Napa can be arranged through specialist wine tour operators or directly with the estate; they require significantly more advance planning.

## Photography in the Cellar

Some estates explicitly prohibit photography in barrel rooms, fermentation halls, and production areas — either for commercial confidentiality or simply as a condition the winemaker has established. The rule is not always posted prominently. When in doubt, ask. A simple "Is it alright to take photos in the cellar?" is a courteous question that every winery host can answer clearly. Photographing without asking in a private production space is poor form. On the other hand, most estates actively welcome photography in the vineyards, exterior buildings, and tasting rooms and understand that visitor photos are free promotion.

## Gift Shop Expectations: Buy Something, Or Not

The gift shop or cellar door is the estate's primary retail outlet, and buying wine there directly supports the producer at a better margin than any retail channel. If you have tasted six wines and enjoyed three, buying a bottle or two of your favourites is a natural expression of appreciation. It is not, however, obligatory — if the wines are not to your taste, a polite departure is entirely appropriate and preferable to purchasing wine under social pressure.

That said, the practice of tasting extensively at a winery — including rare library wines or prestige expressions — and then walking away without any purchase reads as poor form to most producers. The tasting is an investment in hospitality. If the estate's wines fall outside your budget, a simple acknowledgment to your host that you appreciated the tasting goes a long way.

## Tipping Conventions: US Yes, Europe Generally No

In the United States, tipping tasting room staff is standard in California, Oregon, Washington, and most other major wine regions — typically $1-5 per person for a standard tasting, more for extensive time given by a knowledgeable host. The practice has become more widespread as tasting room staff are frequently paid partly on tips, particularly in Napa and Sonoma. Not tipping after a substantive, attentive tasting in an American winery is increasingly noticed.

In Europe, tipping is not expected or standard at winery visits. A small gratuity for exceptional service at a high-end French or Italian estate would not cause offence, but the expectation is absent. The European model is that the tasting fee (where charged) covers the service, and that a purchase is the standard reciprocal act. Applying American tipping norms in European contexts creates awkwardness; accepting European norms in American contexts means poor treatment of hospitality workers.

## The Right Attitude: Curiosity Over Performance

The best winery visits share one quality: genuine curiosity. Hosts who work at estates they love — who made the wine, grew the grapes, or have spent years understanding the estate's history — respond to visitors who ask real questions, engage with what they are tasting, and are honest about what they do and do not like. The worst winery visitor is the one performing expertise they do not have, criticising wines to seem discerning, or name-dropping other estates to establish status.

You do not need to know everything to be a welcome visitor. Honest questions — "What is the difference between these two Pinot Noirs?" or "Which vintage do you think shows the estate's style best?" — produce far better conversations than confident pronouncements. The wine world's greatest pleasure is its inexhaustibility: the most knowledgeable tasters in the world still encounter wines they have never tasted, vineyards they have not visited, varieties they are learning. Approaching a winery visit with that spirit makes the experience better for everyone.
