Sauvignon Blanc is the most instantly recognisable white wine in the
world. Pungent, zesty and unmistakably aromatic, it leaps from the
glass with notes that range from gooseberry and grass to passion
fruit and flint. It is also a chameleon: capable of crisp, mineral
restraint in the Loire, opulent barrel-aged richness in Bordeaux, and
explosive tropical fruit in New Zealand. Few grapes travel so far on
such a distinctive personality.

## A grape of the Loire and Bordeaux

Sauvignon Blanc is native to south-west and central France. In the
Loire Valley it reaches its most elegant, mineral expression; in
Bordeaux it is both a dry-wine and sweet-wine grape, traditionally
blended with Sémillon. DNA studies have shown it to be a parent,
with Cabernet Franc, of Cabernet Sauvignon — a remarkable pedigree
for a white grape. Its name comes from *sauvage*, "wild", a nod to
its vigorous, untamed growth.

## Sancerre and Pouilly-Fumé

The Loire's twin benchmarks sit on opposite banks of the upper river.
Sancerre, on hills of limestone and flinty clay, gives precise,
citrus-and-mineral wines of great purity. Pouilly-Fumé, just across
the water on more flinty soils, gives a smokier, gun-flint-scented
style — the "fumé" in its name. The best growers, such as Didier
Dagueneau (Pouilly-Fumé) and the Vacheron and Cotat families
(Sancerre), make age-worthy wines that defy the grape's reputation for
simplicity.

## White Bordeaux: dry and sweet

In Bordeaux, Sauvignon Blanc is rarely solo. Blended with Sémillon,
it makes the crisp dry whites of Entre-Deux-Mers and the more serious,
barrel-fermented whites of Pessac-Léognan, where estates like Domaine
de Chevalier and Château Smith Haut Lafitte make age-worthy whites of
great complexity. In Sauternes and Barsac, Sauvignon adds acidity and
lift to Sémillon's botrytised sweetness — the structure behind
Château d'Yquem.

## The New Zealand revolution

No region transformed Sauvignon Blanc like Marlborough, at the top of
New Zealand's South Island. First planted commercially in the 1970s,
Marlborough Sauvignon exploded onto the world stage in the 1980s with
a style unlike anything in France: intensely aromatic, bursting with
passion fruit, gooseberry, green capsicum and lime, with searing
acidity. Cloudy Bay, founded in 1985, became its global ambassador;
Greywacke, Dog Point and Seresin are among the quality leaders.

## Other expressions around the world

Sauvignon Blanc thrives wherever cool conditions preserve its
aromatics. South Africa's coastal Elgin and Constantia produce
elegant, restrained versions; Chile's Casablanca and Leyda valleys
make zesty, mineral wines cooled by the Pacific; Austria's Styria
(Steiermark) makes a serious, age-worthy style. California's
"Fumé Blanc" — a name coined by Robert Mondavi in 1968 — denotes an
oak-influenced version.

## Styles and how to taste

Sauvignon Blanc divides broadly into two camps. The unoaked,
stainless-steel style emphasises freshness and aromatics — drink it
young and well chilled. The oaked, often Sémillon-blended style adds
texture, body and ageing potential. Look for the grape's signature
spectrum: green notes (grass, nettle, gooseberry, green pepper) in
cooler climates, tropical notes (passion fruit, guava) in warmer ones,
and a flinty minerality on the best limestone soils.

## Food pairing

Sauvignon Blanc's high acidity and herbaceous edge make it one of the
most food-friendly whites. It is the classic match for goat's cheese
(especially Loire chèvre with Sancerre), and pairs beautifully with
shellfish, ceviche, asparagus, fresh herbs and green salads. The
riper New Zealand style stands up to spicier Asian dishes, while
oaked white Bordeaux can partner roast chicken and richer fish.
