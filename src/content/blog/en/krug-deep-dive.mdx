Krug is the Champagne house most associated with the idea that
multi-vintage blending — rather than vintage purity — can produce
the greatest expression of the appellation. Founded in 1843 by
Joseph Krug, the house has remained in the family until LVMH
acquired it in 1999; the Krug family still drives style under Olivier
Krug.

## The wines

Krug produces six wines, each unusual in some way:

- **Krug Grande Cuvée** — the flagship, a multi-vintage cuvée based
on the harvest of one year, completed with up to 200 reserve wines
from across the previous fifteen years.
- **Krug Rosé** — the only Krug rosé, multi-vintage, blended from
red and white still wines.
- **Krug Vintage** — released only in great years, around eight years
after harvest.
- **Krug Collection** — older vintages from the cellar, released
when Krug believes they have reached a second peak.
- **Krug Clos du Mesnil** — single-vineyard Blanc de Blancs from a
1.85-hectare walled vineyard in Mesnil-sur-Oger. Among the world's
most expensive Champagnes.
- **Krug Clos d'Ambonnay** — single-vineyard Blanc de Noirs (Pinot
Noir) from a 0.68-hectare walled vineyard in Ambonnay. The rarest
Krug.

## The oak vinification

Unusually for a major Champagne house, Krug ferments every base wine
in small (205-litre) oak barrels — many of them old, with the same
barrel sometimes used for decades. This adds a distinctive oxidative
edge to the wines.

## The reserve wine library

Krug holds reserve wines from many vintages, with the largest single
component in any Grande Cuvée typically representing only 30-40% of
the blend. The reserve library is among the most valuable in
Champagne and the basis of the house's signature style.

## The ID number

Since 2011, every bottle of Krug carries an ID number on the back
label. Entering it on Krug's website (or app) reveals the
composition, base year, and disgorgement date of the cuvée.

## Visiting

Krug's headquarters in Reims (5 Rue Coquebert) does not accept
casual visitors. Allocations and private visits are arranged
through the trade or through the Krug Society. The Champagne, when
encountered, is best drunk from a white-wine glass rather than a
flute.
