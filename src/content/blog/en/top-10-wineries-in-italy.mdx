Italy is simultaneously the world's largest wine producer by volume and home to some of its most complex, age-worthy, and historically significant wines. With 20 wine-producing regions, over 350 authorised DOC and DOCG appellations, and an almost inexhaustible range of indigenous varieties, Italy rewards a lifetime of study. The ten estates here are the names that matter most at the apex of Italian wine — the producers whose decisions have shaped how the country is seen by the world and how it sees itself.

## 1. Antinori, Tuscany

The Antinori family has produced wine since 1385, making it one of the longest-continuously-operating wine businesses in history. But the estate's modern significance was established in 1971 when Piero Antinori and winemaker Giacomo Tachis created Tignanello: a Sangiovese-based wine with Cabernet Sauvignon blended in — a violation of the rules of Chianti Classico at the time — aged in small barriques rather than the large Slavonian oak botti traditional in Tuscany. Tignanello was released as a simple Vino da Tavola (table wine) but won immediate critical acclaim and launched the Super Tuscan movement. Solaia — 75% Cabernet Sauvignon, 20% Cabernet Franc, 5% Sangiovese from the same Tignanello estate — is the prestige red, and the 1997 and 2001 vintages are canonical Super Tuscans. Antinori also owns Ornellaia (jointly), Pèppoli, and the Tignanello estate in the Chianti Classico heartland.

## 2. Gaja, Piedmont

Angelo Gaja transformed Barbaresco — and arguably all of Italian fine wine — through a series of decisions in the 1970s and 1980s that were considered heretical at the time: introducing small French oak barriques, reducing yields dramatically, bottling single-vineyard wines (Sorì San Lorenzo, Sorì Tildìn, Costa Russi) from a DOCG that had no tradition of named-vineyard bottlings, and charging prices that placed his wines alongside first-growth Bordeaux. The result was a complete revaluation of Nebbiolo and of northern Italian wine internationally. The Gaja Barbaresco DOCG (the declassified, multi-vineyard blend — Gaja removed its single-vineyard wines from the Barbaresco DOCG in 1996 to add 5% Barbera without losing quality designation) and the Barolo Sperss and Conteisa from the Serralunga and La Morra communes respectively are the flagships. Gaja's Rossj-Bass (Chardonnay) and Gaia & Rey (Chardonnay) demonstrated that Piedmont could produce world-class whites.

## 3. Tenuta San Guido (Sassicaia), Bolgheri

Sassicaia, the wine that created the Bolgheri DOC, was first produced commercially in 1968 by Mario Incisa della Rocchetta after private experimentation with Cabernet Sauvignon on the Tuscan coast since the 1940s. The inspiration was overtly Bordelais — Marchese Incisa wanted to make a wine like the great Médoc — and the outcome, a Cabernet Sauvignon-Cabernet Franc blend from coastal Bolgheri, was so extraordinary that Bolgheri Sassicaia DOC was eventually created specifically for a single wine from a single producer. The 1985 vintage was named best Cabernet Sauvignon in the world at a blind tasting of 33 vintages by Decanter; the 1988, 2016, and 2018 are the modern benchmarks. Tenuta San Guido's second wine, Le Difese (Cabernet Sauvignon-Sangiovese), offers the estate's quality at an accessible price.

## 4. Ornellaia, Bolgheri

Ornellaia, founded by Lodovico Antinori in 1981 and now owned by the Frescobaldi family, is Bolgheri's second iconic estate. The flagship Ornellaia (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc, Petit Verdot) is produced from the estate's 100-hectare Bolgheri property and consistently ranks among Italy's top five reds. The Masseto single-vineyard Merlot — from a distinctive clay-rich parcel within the estate — has been produced separately since 1986 and, since 2019, as an independent estate. The 2001, 2006, and 2015 Ornellaia vintages are the critical reference points. Le Serre Nuove dell'Ornellaia (the second wine) and Bolgheri Bianco Le Volte round out the portfolio.

## 5. Biondi-Santi, Montalcino

Brunello di Montalcino DOCG is arguably Italy's greatest red wine denomination, and Biondi-Santi is its founding estate. Ferruccio Biondi-Santi isolated a particular clone of Sangiovese Grosso (now called the Brunello clone) in the late 19th century, vinified it as a single-variety wine without blending, and aged it in large Slavonian oak botti for multiple years. The result was a wine capable of ageing for decades or centuries. The 1888 and 1891 vintages, still occasionally tasted, are the oldest documented bottles of Brunello; the Riserva (produced only in exceptional years and requiring at least 6 years ageing) is among the most collectible Italian wines. Il Greppo, the estate in Montalcino, is the most historically significant address in Tuscany.

## 6. Frescobaldi, Tuscany

The Frescobaldi family has produced wine in Tuscany since the 14th century and is now one of Italy's largest premium wine groups, owning ten Tuscan estates including Ornellaia, Masseto, and Castelgiocondo (Brunello di Montalcino). The flagship Frescobaldi estate in the traditional sense is Tenuta Castello di Nipozzano in Chianti Rufina — one of the most underrated sub-appellations of Chianti, producing wines of great freshness, acidity, and longevity from higher-altitude Sangiovese. The Nipozzano Riserva is the benchmark; the Mormoreto (Cabernet Sauvignon from Nipozzano) is the prestige Super Tuscan expression. Castelgiocondo Brunello and the Luce della Vite joint venture (with Mondavi) in Montalcino represent the wider portfolio.

## 7. Ceretto, Piedmont

The Ceretto family estate, based in Alba, produces some of the finest Barolo and Barbaresco in Piedmont from holdings in the top Langhe communes. The Barolo Bricco Rocche from Castiglione Falletto and the Barolo Brunate from La Morra are the prestige expressions — both single-vineyard wines of great complexity and longevity. The Barbaresco Asij from Treiso and the Langhe Arneis Blangé are important expressions of the estate's range. The iconic Acino label, designed by Sol LeWitt, and the Cube (a glass pavilion in the Bricco Rocche vineyard) are part of Ceretto's significant investment in art and wine tourism in the Langhe.

## 8. Vietti, Barolo

Luca Currado of Vietti is one of Barolo's most respected winemakers, producing single-vineyard Barolo from some of the appellation's most acclaimed crus: Rocche di Castiglione (from Castiglione Falletto), Brunate (La Morra), Lazzarito (Serralunga d'Alba), Villero (Castiglione Falletto). Each wine expresses a different face of Nebbiolo — the power and tar of Serralunga, the elegance and roses of La Morra, the structured minerality of Castiglione Falletto — making Vietti's range a study in Barolo terroir. The Castiglione blend (a multi-village Barolo) and the excellent Barbera d'Asti Tre Vigne are the accessible entry points. Vietti's Barbera bottlings are among Piedmont's finest expressions of the variety.

## 9. Giuseppe Mascarello e Figlio, Barolo

Mauro Mascarello's estate produces one of the absolute benchmarks of traditional Barolo: the Monprivato single-vineyard Barolo from the Castiglione Falletto commune, made from a single plot of Michet clone Nebbiolo (an older, lower-yielding mutation) in the traditional manner — long maceration, large oak botti, no new wood. The Monprivato is produced in almost every vintage and ages magnificently for 30 years or more; the Riserva Ca' d'Morissio (from the oldest Michet vines, produced only in exceptional years) is among the most coveted Barolo produced. Mascarello is a touchstone for the traditionalist school in Barolo — the estate that most clearly embodies the argument that great Barolo requires patience, not modernity.

## 10. Allegrini, Valpolicella and Verona

Allegrini, the most important quality producer in Valpolicella, makes Amarone della Valpolicella DOCG of extraordinary concentration and structure from a combination of estate vineyards in the Classico zone and the hillside La Grola and Fieramonte single-vineyard parcels. Amarone is produced from partially dried (appassimento) Corvina, Rondinella, and Molinara grapes — a traditional method producing wine of 15-16% ABV, near-black colour, chocolate, dried cherry, and roasted meat complexity, with a lifespan of thirty or more years for the finest examples. The Allegrini Amarone from the 1997, 2003, and 2015 vintages are the modern reference points. La Poja, a single-variety Corvina not subjected to appassimento, is the estate's most distinctive innovation.

## The Super Tuscan Revolution

Tignanello (1971) and Sassicaia (1968) created the Super Tuscan movement by demonstrating that Italian wine produced outside the rules of the DOC system could be more complex and age-worthy than anything the approved appellations then produced. Their example inspired a generation of producers to plant Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Syrah in Tuscany, and the critical acclaim they received in the 1980s forced the Italian appellation system to evolve — eventually creating the Bolgheri DOC, revising Chianti Classico rules to permit international varieties, and introducing the IGT (Indicazione Geografica Tipica) category that allowed serious wines to be made outside traditional DOC boundaries.
