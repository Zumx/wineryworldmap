The French word *terroir* is used liberally in wine writing, often
as a shorthand for "place-specific character." That is not wrong,
but it understates what is meant. Terroir is a working hypothesis
about why a wine made from a specific patch of ground tastes
different from one made down the slope, across the road, or in the
next valley — and unpacking the components is more useful than
repeating the word.

## Geology and soil

The first component is what sits under the vine. Bedrock — granite,
limestone, schist, basalt — and the topsoil derived from it
influence drainage, the mineral profile available to the roots,
and the depth that roots can penetrate. The famous regional
distinctions of European wine often track geology closely:
Burgundy's gold-and-silver slope is limestone with marl bands;
Mosel's slate; Châteauneuf's *galets roulés* river-rolled stones;
the granite of Beaujolais; the volcanic basalt of Etna. Each
produces a measurably different wine from the same grape.

## Microclimate

The second component is the local climate — not the regional
average, but the actual conditions at the specific vineyard. South-
facing slopes ripen more fully than north-facing ones in the
Northern Hemisphere. A vineyard at 350 metres is meaningfully
cooler than one at 100 metres in the same valley. A site sheltered
by a hill ridge from afternoon wind ripens differently from one
exposed to it. Within Burgundy, the Côte de Nuits and the Côte
de Beaune just kilometres apart give recognisably different reds
because of these differences.

## Aspect, slope, and elevation

Closely related but worth separating: how steep is the slope, in
which direction does it face, and how high is it above sea level.
Steeper slopes drain better but cost more to farm; south-facing
sites accumulate heat; higher altitudes preserve acidity in
warmer regions. The same Cabernet planted on a south-facing
gravelly slope, a north-facing limestone bench, and a flat clay
plain in the same village will produce three different wines.

## Water dynamics

Often underweighted in popular accounts. The total annual rainfall
matters less than when it falls (a wet spring followed by a dry
late summer is ideal); the soil's water-holding capacity matters
more than rainfall (deep clay can sustain vines through dry years
even with low rainfall); and the proximity of rivers, lakes, or
sea moderates temperature extremes. Latour's clay sub-stratum and
the maritime influence on Bordeaux's left bank are both terroir
features in this water sense.

## The human factor

The hotly contested question: should the work of the grower be
part of terroir or separate from it? French tradition typically
includes the "traditional human practice" — pruning style,
training system, planting density, the choice of grape variety
itself — as part of terroir, on the grounds that the long human
relationship with the land shapes what can be expressed. Newer
World thinking often holds the natural physical features as
terroir and the human work as winemaking. The two positions are
not really contradictory.

## How terroir shows in the glass

A taster cannot reverse-engineer geology from a wine, but
characteristic terroir signatures do come through. Mosel slate
shows as a particular mineral cut in mature Riesling; Burgundian
limestone shows as the chalky texture and oxidative-but-stable
white wine profile; Etna volcanic basalt shows in the smoky
salinity of mature Nerello Mascalese. These signatures take
years of comparative tasting to recognise and can be subtle —
masked by oak treatment, ripeness, or winemaking choice.

## Terroir versus brand

The term is sometimes used to elevate marketing claims. A wine
made from purchased grapes at varying sources, aged the same way
year on year, and finished to a house style is not a terroir
wine in any meaningful sense — even if the label says it is. A
genuine terroir wine is one in which the same producer, working
the same site, makes a recognisably different wine in different
vintages because the vintage talks through the site.

## The most terroir-driven categories

Some wine categories are more terroir-driven than others by
construction. Burgundy is the global reference, with hierarchies of
parcels at the village, premier cru, and grand cru level all tied
to specific named plots. Barolo and Barbaresco operate similarly
with their MGA system. Mosel Riesling is named by single
vineyards. By contrast, many large-volume New World wines — and
mass-market Champagne — are deliberately constructed across many
sites for stylistic consistency, which is a different goal.
