Syrah and Shiraz are the same grape, but the two names have come to
signify two philosophies. Call it Syrah and you usually mean the
cool, peppery, savoury reds of France's northern Rhône and their
imitators; call it Shiraz and you mean the ripe, plush, fruit-forward
wines of Australia. Between those poles lies one of the world's most
versatile and rewarding red grapes — and a fascinating lesson in how
place shapes a wine.

## Origins in the northern Rhône

DNA analysis has established that Syrah is a natural cross of two
obscure south-eastern French grapes, Dureza and Mondeuse Blanche,
making it firmly French in origin despite romantic legends of Persian
or Sicilian roots. Its spiritual home is the northern Rhône, where it
is the only permitted red grape in appellations such as
Côte-Rôtie, Hermitage, Cornas and Saint-Joseph.

## The northern Rhône style

Here Syrah grows on steep, terraced granite and schist slopes that
demand hand labour. The wines are medium-bodied, firmly structured
and aromatic, with signature notes of black pepper, violet, olive,
smoked meat and blackcurrant. Côte-Rôtie, where a little white
Viognier may be co-fermented, is the most perfumed; Hermitage the
most powerful and long-lived; Cornas the most rustic and dark. These
are wines of restraint and savour rather than sheer fruit.

## The southern Rhône and GSM

In the warmer southern Rhône, Syrah rarely stands alone. It joins
Grenache and Mourvèdre in the famous GSM blends of
Châteauneuf-du-Pape, Gigondas and the broader Côtes du Rhône, where
it lends colour, structure and spice to Grenache's generous fruit.
This blending role has been exported worldwide, from Spain's Priorat
to California's Central Coast.

## Shiraz in Australia

Syrah reached Australia in the 1830s and, as Shiraz, became the
country's signature grape. In the warm Barossa Valley and McLaren
Vale of South Australia it produces rich, full-bodied, opulent wines
of dark plum, blackberry, liquorice and chocolate, often with sweet
oak. Penfolds Grange, first made in 1951, is the icon. Some of the
world's oldest surviving vines — pre-phylloxera Shiraz planted in the
1840s and 1860s — still bear fruit in the Barossa.

## Cool-climate Australian Shiraz

Australia is not only warmth. In cooler regions the grape turns back
toward its Rhône character: the Grampians and Heathcote in Victoria,
the Canberra District (where Clonakilla's Shiraz-Viognier is a
benchmark), the Adelaide Hills, and the granite-soiled Great Southern
of Western Australia all make peppery, medium-bodied, spicy Shiraz
that bridges the gap between the two worlds.

## Syrah around the world

The grape has spread far. Washington State's Rocks District produces
intense, savoury Syrah; California's Central Coast has a serious Rhône
movement led by producers like Sine Qua Non and Alban. South Africa's
Swartland makes characterful, old-vine Syrah; Chile's cooler coastal
and high zones, and a handful of growers in the northern Rhône style
across Italy and Spain, complete a global picture of remarkable
diversity.

## Tasting Syrah and Shiraz

The grape's calling cards are colour and aromatic complexity. Look
for deep purple-black hues; aromas spanning blackberry and plum,
black and white pepper, violet, olive tapenade, smoked meat and, with
bottle age, leather and game. Cool-climate examples emphasise pepper
and savour with firm acidity; warm-climate examples emphasise ripe
fruit, fuller body and softer tannins. Both reward decanting and pair
superbly with grilled and roasted meats, game and hard cheeses.
