Barolo is made from a single grape, Nebbiolo, grown on a small
patch of hills in Piedmont's Langhe, and yet it is among the most
complex and long-lived red wines in the world. Pale in colour but
ferocious in tannin and acidity, it ages for decades and smells of
tar, roses, dried cherry and truffle. Understanding Barolo means
understanding its communes, its crus, and the long debate between
tradition and modernity that has shaped it.

## The Nebbiolo grape

Nebbiolo is a difficult, late-ripening variety that buds early and
harvests late, often into late October when fog — *nebbia*, the root
of its name — settles over the Langhe hills. It demands the best
south-facing sites and reaches full ripeness only in this corner of
Piedmont and a few neighbouring zones. Its hallmark is the
combination of high tannin, high acidity and relatively light
colour — a structure that allows extraordinary ageing while never
becoming heavy.

## The DOCG rules

Barolo received DOC status in 1966 and was elevated to DOCG —
Denominazione di Origine Controllata e Garantita, Italy's highest
tier — in 1980. The rules require 100% Nebbiolo, a minimum of 38
months of ageing (of which at least 18 in wood) before release, and
62 months for the Riserva. The wines come exclusively from
designated hillside vineyards; the valley floors are excluded.

## The eleven communes

Barolo is produced in eleven communes, but five dominate. Barolo
and La Morra, on Tortonian soils of calcareous marl, give the more
perfumed, accessible, earlier-maturing style. Serralunga d'Alba,
Monforte d'Alba and Castiglione Falletto, on the older Helvetian
soils with more sandstone, give the most structured, powerful and
slow-maturing wines. A traditional blended Barolo drew on several
communes; today single-vineyard bottlings are the norm.

## The MGA cru system

Since 2010, Barolo has had an official system of named vineyards
called Menzioni Geografiche Aggiuntive (MGA) — additional geographic
mentions, often loosely called crus. There are around 170 of them.
The most celebrated include Cannubi in Barolo, Brunate and Rocche
dell'Annunziata in La Morra, Vigna Rionda and Lazzarito in
Serralunga, Bussia and Ginestra in Monforte, and Monprivato and
Villero in Castiglione Falletto. A vineyard name on the label now
carries real geographic meaning.

## Tradition versus modernity

For decades, Barolo was divided into two camps. Traditionalists —
Giuseppe Rinaldi, Bartolo Mascarello, Giacomo Conterno — favoured
long macerations and ageing in large old Slavonian oak botti,
producing pale, austere, hugely long-lived wines. Modernists, led by
producers like Elio Altare and Domenico Clerico from the late 1970s,
shortened macerations and used small French barriques for darker,
softer, earlier-drinking wines. The "Barolo wars" have since cooled;
most estates now sit somewhere in between.

## The great producers

Giacomo Conterno, in Monforte, makes the legendary Monfortino
Riserva from Serralunga's Cascina Francia — perhaps the single most
revered Barolo. Bartolo Mascarello and Giuseppe Rinaldi remain the
purest traditionalists. Bruno Giacosa, the great solitary genius of
Neive, made profound Barolo and Barbaresco until his death in 2018.
Vietti, Giacomo Borgogno, Roberto Voerzio, Elio Grasso, Luciano
Sandrone and Paolo Scavino are among the other reference names. Gaja,
though more famous for Barbaresco, also makes Barolo from Serralunga
and La Morra.

## Vintages and ageing

Barolo is a wine for patience. A serious bottle rarely shows well
before its tenth birthday and the best vintages improve for thirty
years or more. Celebrated recent years include 2010, 2013, 2016 and
2019 — all marked by the balance of ripeness and acidity that
Nebbiolo needs. Warmer years like 2011 and 2015 produced more
forward wines. When young, Barolo benefits from hours of decanting;
when mature, it should be handled gently to preserve its delicate
sediment and fading colour.

## Visiting the Barolo zone

The town of Alba is the natural base, famous also for its white
truffles each autumn. The villages of Barolo, La Morra, Serralunga
and Monforte are a short drive apart through a UNESCO-listed
landscape of vine-covered hills. La Morra's belvedere offers the
classic panorama across the whole appellation. Many estates welcome
visitors by appointment, and the village enotecas — especially the
regional enoteca in the Castello Falletto di Barolo — offer broad
tastings without prior contacts.
