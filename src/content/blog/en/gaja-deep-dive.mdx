Gaja is the family estate that, more than any other, repositioned
Italian wine on the world stage in the 1970s and 1980s. Based in
the small town of Barbaresco in the Langhe hills of Piedmont, Gaja
is famous for an uncompromising approach to viticulture, a series
of single-vineyard Barbaresco wines, and prices that began the
era of Italian luxury wine.

## The family

Founded in 1859 by Giovanni Gaja, the estate is now run by Gaia,
Rossana, and Giovanni Gaja, fourth and fifth generations of the
family. Angelo Gaja — their father — defined the modern era from
the 1970s onward and remains an outsized figure in Italian fine
wine.

## The revolution

Angelo Gaja's decisions in the 1970s and 1980s were considered
heretical at the time:

- Introducing small French barriques alongside the traditional
large Slavonian botti.
- Reducing yields dramatically.
- Bottling single-vineyard wines from Barbaresco (Sorì San
Lorenzo, Sorì Tildìn, Costa Russi) at a time when no DOCG allowed
named-vineyard bottlings.
- Charging prices that placed Gaja alongside first-growth Bordeaux.

## The Sorì wines

- **Sorì San Lorenzo** — first vintage 1967. The most powerful of
the three.
- **Sorì Tildìn** — first vintage 1970. Named for Angelo's
grandmother. Considered the most elegant.
- **Costa Russi** — first vintage 1978. From a south-facing slope.

In 1996, Gaja removed all three from the Barbaresco DOCG so he
could add 5% Barbera without losing quality designation. They are
now bottled as Langhe DOC.

## The other estates

The Gaja portfolio extends well beyond Barbaresco:

- **Gaja Barbaresco DOCG** — the main wine, now a multi-vineyard
blend.
- **Sperss** — Barolo from Serralunga d'Alba, since 1988.
- **Conteisa** — Barolo from La Morra.
- **Gaia & Rey** — Chardonnay, the first serious white from the
estate, first vintage 1983.
- **Pieve Santa Restituta** — Brunello di Montalcino estate
(Sugarille and Rennina single-vineyards) acquired in 1994.
- **Ca' Marcanda** — a Bolgheri estate acquired in 1996, producing
Camarcanda, Magari, and Promis.

## Visiting

Gaja has historically not accepted casual visitors. Some structured
visits can be arranged through the trade; the estate's
headquarters in the village of Barbaresco are a private property.
Tasting at quality Piedmont restaurants and the Barbaresco village
enoteca is the realistic route.
