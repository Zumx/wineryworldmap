Sherry is the most misunderstood great wine in the world. Mention it
and many people picture a sweet, oxidised drink in a dusty bottle —
yet the finest Sherries are bone dry, electric with salt and acidity,
and among the best value in fine wine. Made around the town of Jerez
de la Frontera in Andalusia, Sherry is defined by two phenomena found
almost nowhere else: a living veil of yeast called *flor*, and the
fractional-blending system known as the solera.

## The Sherry Triangle

Sherry comes from a defined zone in the far south-west of Spain, the
"Sherry Triangle" between Jerez de la Frontera, Sanlúcar de
Barrameda and El Puerto de Santa María. The defining soil is
*albariza*, a brilliant white, chalk-rich earth that reflects the
fierce Andalusian sun and retains winter rain deep below the
surface. The Palomino grape, neutral and high-yielding, is the base
of almost all dry Sherry.

## Flor: the living veil

After the base wine is fermented dry and lightly fortified to around
15%, something extraordinary happens in the cask: a thick film of
yeast, the flor, grows across the wine's surface. This living veil
consumes oxygen, glycerol and acetic acid, protecting the wine from
oxidation and giving it a piercing, savoury, almost briny character.
Wines aged entirely under flor are "biologically aged"; those that
lose their flor age oxidatively. This fork defines the whole range.

## Fino and Manzanilla

The driest, palest, most delicate Sherries. Fino is aged under flor
in the bodegas of Jerez and El Puerto; Manzanilla is the same style
aged in the cooler, more humid coastal air of Sanlúcar de Barrameda,
where the flor grows thickest and the wine takes on a distinctive
saline tang. Both are best drunk fresh and lightly chilled, and they
are sublime with the fried fish, olives and jamón of Andalusia.

## Amontillado and Palo Cortado

When a Fino's flor eventually dies — naturally or by further
fortification to around 17% — the wine begins to age oxidatively,
deepening to amber and developing nutty, savoury complexity. This is
Amontillado: a wine that began under flor and finished in air. Palo
Cortado is a rare, prized accident — a wine with the finesse of an
Amontillado and the body of an Oloroso, traditionally a Fino that
lost its flor unexpectedly.

## Oloroso

Fortified from the start to around 18% so that flor never forms,
Oloroso ages entirely oxidatively, growing dark, rich and powerful
with aromas of walnut, leather and dried fruit. It is naturally dry,
despite its weight, and the finest old Olorosos are among the most
concentrated wines in existence. Sweetened versions sold as "cream"
Sherry are blends, not a natural style.

## Pedro Ximénez and sweet styles

The great sweet Sherry is made from Pedro Ximénez (PX) grapes laid
out in the sun to raisin, concentrating their sugar to staggering
levels. Aged in solera, PX becomes almost black, viscous as treacle,
tasting of fig, date, raisin and coffee. Moscatel makes a lighter
sweet wine. These are dessert wines of extraordinary intensity,
superb over vanilla ice cream.

## The solera system

No vintage Sherry exists in the ordinary sense, because Sherry is
blended across years by the solera. Casks are stacked in tiers; wine
for bottling is drawn from the oldest tier (the *solera* proper),
which is topped up from the tier above (the first *criadera*), and so
on up the stack. Young wine is thus continually blended into old,
producing a consistent house style and an average age that can reach
decades — VOS and VORS designations indicate wines averaging over 20
and 30 years respectively.

## The great bodegas

Jerez is dominated by historic houses with cathedral-like cellars.
González Byass (home of Tío Pepe Fino), Lustau, Hidalgo-La Gitana
(the Manzanilla benchmark in Sanlúcar), Barbadillo, Valdespino,
Equipo Navazos and the legendary Bodegas Tradición all welcome
visitors. A tour through the towering arched naves, with the flor
visible on the wine, is one of the great wine-tourism experiences in
Spain.
