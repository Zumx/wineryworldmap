Spain is the country with the largest planted vineyard area in the world, and its wine quality has undergone a transformation as dramatic as any in the past forty years. The story runs from Rioja's 19th-century Bordeaux influence to Ribera del Duero's emergence as a rival, through Priorat's meteoric rise from near-abandonment to one of the world's most discussed wine terroirs, and into the sherry bodegas of Jerez where some of the most complex and undervalued wines on Earth age under layers of biological yeast. These ten bodegas define Spanish wine's current standing.

## 1. Bodegas Vega Sicilia, Ribera del Duero

Vega Sicilia is Spain's most legendary wine estate and producer of Unico — a wine considered by many to be Spain's greatest red. Located in Valbuena de Duero on the banks of the River Duero, the estate was founded in 1864 and planted with Tinto Fino (Tempranillo) alongside Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Malbec. Unico is released only when the cellar team judges it ready — typically between 10 and 25 years after vintage, following maturation in a succession of different oak vessels. The 1962, 1968, and 1970 Unico vintages are the canonical modern benchmarks; recent releases of the 1996 and 1999 have confirmed the wine's extraordinary ageing potential. Valbuena 5° (the estate's second wine, released at 5 years) and Alión (a more modern-style Tempranillo from the same ownership) complete the portfolio.

## 2. Tinto Pesquera, Ribera del Duero

Alejandro Fernández's Tinto Pesquera was the wine that first brought Ribera del Duero to international attention, when American critic Robert Parker compared the 1982 vintage to Petrus in 1986 — a comparison that triggered a rush of investment into the then-obscure region. The Pesquera Reserva and Gran Reserva from 100% Tempranillo (Tinto Fino) are benchmarks of the appellation's traditional style: aged in American oak, tannic, structured, and built for long ageing. Fernández's Condado de Haza estate in Roa de Duero produces a more modern-styled Tempranillo from younger vineyards. The two estates together represent the range of stylistic ambition within Ribera del Duero's Tempranillo.

## 3. Marqués de Riscal, Rioja

Marqués de Riscal, founded in 1858 in Elciego, Rioja Alavesa, is one of the oldest and most historically significant bodegas in Spain. The Rioja Reserva and Gran Reserva (predominantly Tempranillo, with Graciano and Mazuelo) are the traditional flagships; the single-vineyard Baron de Chirel (Tempranillo and Cabernet Sauvignon) is the prestige modern expression. The bodega's international fame has been amplified by Frank Gehry's titanium-sheet hotel building (designed 2003, opened 2006) — a spectacular piece of architecture directly above the winery cellars that has become one of the most photographed winery buildings in the world. The Marqués de Riscal Hotel and spa complex makes the estate one of Spain's most complete wine tourism destinations.

## 4. Marqués de Murrieta, Rioja

The Marqués de Murrieta estate in Ygay, Logroño, claims to be the oldest continuously operating bodega in Rioja, founded in 1852 by Luciano de Murrieta. The Castillo Ygay Gran Reserva Especial — produced only in exceptional years, aged for a minimum of two years in oak and ten years in bottle, often released 20 or more years after vintage — is one of the most distinctive wines in Spain: oxidative, complex, walnut and dried fruit in the Rioja traditional style, capable of aging for half a century. The Dalmau (a modern-styled Tempranillo with Cabernet Sauvignon and Graciano) and the Capellanía (single-vineyard white Rioja) demonstrate the estate's range beyond its traditional expression.

## 5. R. López de Heredia, Rioja

López de Heredia in Haro is the most committed guardian of traditional Rioja winemaking — the bodega where nothing has fundamentally changed since its founding in 1877 and where wines are aged for years or decades in American oak before release. The Tondonia Reserva and Gran Reserva (Tempranillo, Garnacha, Mazuelo, Graciano from the single Viña Tondonia vineyard above the Ebro) are the reference wines for traditional Rioja: oxidative, brick-coloured with age, complex with walnut, leather, and dried cherry character. The Tondonia white Gran Reserva (Viura-dominant) is perhaps the world's most distinctive aged white wine — the 1970 white is still in production and worth seeking out. López de Heredia does not receive casual visitors and functions more as a shrine than a commercial tourist operation.

## 6. Bodegas Roda, Rioja

Roda, established by Mario Rotllant in 1987 in a historic bodega in Haro's barrio de la estación (the railway station district, surrounded by historic Rioja estates), produces the benchmark modern-classical Rioja — wines that honour the region's Tempranillo tradition while adopting modern viticultural standards (low yields, careful selection) and restrained French oak ageing. Roda I Reserva and Roda Reserva are the primary flagship expressions; Cirsion — from old low-yield Graciano vines, produced in very small quantities — is the cult wine of the estate and one of the finest single-variety Graciano produced anywhere. Roda has become the model for Rioja producers who want quality modernity without abandoning the region's character.

## 7. Bodegas Muga, Rioja

Muga, also in Haro's station quarter, is the estate most committed to traditional Rioja vinification including the use of egg whites for fining and the maturation of all wines in oak. The Muga Reserva and Selección Especial Reserva are the core quality expressions; Torre Muga (Tempranillo with Merlot and Mazuelo, aged in French and American new oak) is the prestige modern red. Prado Enea Gran Reserva — from old vines, long-aged in barrel and bottle — is the estate's most traditional and age-worthy wine. Muga's own cooperage, producing American and French oak barrels within the bodega, is unique in Rioja and a visible demonstration of the estate's commitment to craft production.

## 8. Mas Doix, Priorat

The Doix family estate in Poboleda, at the centre of the Priorat DOCa (one of only two DOCa — the highest Spanish designation — alongside Rioja), farms some of the appellation's oldest Garnacha and Cariñena vines on the distinctive llicorella (layered slate and quartz) soils that define Priorat terroir. Salanques (old-vine Garnacha-Cariñena blend) and Costers de Vinyes Velles (old-vine, lower-yield, prestige expression) are the flagship wines — mineral, dark, tar-and-black-fruit, with the massive structure that llicorella produces. Mas Doix represents the smaller-estate tradition within Priorat alongside the estates that attracted the most initial investment and publicity in the 1990s.

## 9. Álvaro Palacios, Priorat

Álvaro Palacios arrived in the Priorat in 1989 from the Palacios Remondo family estate in Rioja, having trained in Château Pétrus and been inspired by the potential of Priorat's abandoned slate hillside vineyards. L'Ermita — a single-plot Garnacha from the steepest slate terraces of La Vilella Baixa, first produced in 1993 — is now one of the most expensive and most discussed wines produced in Spain, regularly achieving near-perfect scores and trading on the secondary market for prices comparable to classified Bordeaux. Finca Dofí (old-vine Garnacha-Cariñena) and Les Terrasses (entry Priorat) complete the estate's hierarchy. Palacios's nephew Ricardo Pérez Palacios manages the acclaimed Bierzo estate Descendientes de J. Palacios, producing benchmark Mencía from ancient vines in Galicia.

## 10. Codorníu, Penedès

Codorníu, founded in 1551 in Sant Sadurní d'Anoia, is the world's oldest family-owned wine business and the producer of the first Spanish Cava — made in 1872 by Josep Raventós Fatjó using the Champagne method. The modernista cellar buildings designed by Josep Puig i Cadafalch (1904, now a national monument) are among the finest wine architecture in Europe. Anna de Codorníu (Chardonnay-based, prestige Cava) and Codorníu Reina María Cristina Gran Reserva are the top-tier Cavas; the Jaume de Codorníu and Non Plus Ultra vintage expressions demonstrate the category's capacity for aged complexity. Codorníu's scale (it produces 40 million bottles of Cava annually) has not prevented it from maintaining genuine quality in its prestige tier.

## Sherry: The World's Most Complex and Undervalued Wine

No account of Spanish wine is complete without Sherry. González Byass's Tío Pepe Fino — the world's most recognised dry Sherry, fresh and saline under a thick layer of protective flor yeast — and the same house's Noé Pedro Ximénez (a near-black dessert wine of almost infinite sweetness) represent the extremes of the Sherry range. Equipo Navazos, the project that selects exceptional single barrels from Jerez, Sanlúcar, and Montilla-Moriles, has produced some of the finest individual Sherry expressions ever bottled. Hidalgo La Gitana Manzanilla, from the sea-cooled seaside bodegas of Sanlúcar de Barrameda, is the most consistently elegant expression of the saline, delicate Manzanilla style. Sherry deserves far more attention than the wine world currently gives it.
