Tempranillo is Spain's most planted red grape and the engine of
Rioja, Ribera del Duero, and Toro. Each region produces a
recognisably different wine from the same variety — Rioja's American-
oak Reserva and Gran Reserva style, Ribera's denser French-oak
plateau wines, and Toro's hot-climate concentration.

## Rioja Alta: the classicists

R. López de Heredia (Viña Tondonia), La Rioja Alta (Gran Reserva
904), CVNE (Imperial), Marqués de Murrieta (Castillo Ygay), Bodegas
Muga (Prado Enea), and Marqués de Riscal. American oak, long oak
and bottle ageing, only released when ready.

## Rioja Alavesa: the modernists

Artadi (Viña El Pisón), Remírez de Ganuza, and Sierra Cantabria
(Viñedos Sierra Cantabria) represent the modern, single-vineyard
movement on the Alavesa side of the Ebro.

## Ribera del Duero

Vega Sicilia (Único, Reserva Especial), Dominio de Pingus, Aalto,
Hacienda Monasterio, Tinto Pesquera, Alión, and Pago de Carraovejas
are reference Ribera estates. French oak dominates here.

## Toro

Tinta de Toro (the local Tempranillo) produces the most powerful
Spanish wines. Numanthia (Termanthia), Bodega Pintia (Vega Sicilia's
Toro outpost), Maurodos (San Roman), and Elias Mora are leading
producers.

## Vinos de Pago and miscellaneous Castilla

Pago de Carraovejas straddles Ribera and the Vinos de Pago system.
Mauro VS, Telmo Rodríguez (especially Pegaso), and Bodegas Leda
operate outside the strict DO system in Castilla y León VT.

## Portugal: Tinta Roriz

Tempranillo is widely grown across the Iberian peninsula — including
in the Douro Valley, where it's called Tinta Roriz and figures
prominently in dry red Douro and Port blends from estates like
Quinta do Crasto, Quinta do Vale Meão, and Niepoort.

## Beyond Spain

Tempranillo is grown commercially in Argentina (Bodegas Bressia,
Familia Schroeder), Mexico (LA Cetto), the US Pacific Northwest
(Abacela in Oregon), and Australia (Mount Majura in Canberra and
Tar & Roses in Victoria) — though none yet rivals the Iberian
reference points.
