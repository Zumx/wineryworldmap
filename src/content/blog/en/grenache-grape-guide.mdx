Grenache is the great grape of the warm Mediterranean — sweet,
generous and high in alcohol, with a pale colour that belies its
power. It is the backbone of Châteauneuf-du-Pape, the engine of
Spain's Priorat revival, and the source of some of the oldest vines
on Earth in Australia's Barossa. Long dismissed as a mere blending
grape, Grenache has lately been rediscovered as a vehicle for some of
the most exciting fine wines in the world.

## A grape of Spanish origin

Grenache — Garnacha in its native Spain — almost certainly originated
in the north-east of the Iberian Peninsula, in Aragón, before
spreading across the western Mediterranean. It is one of the most
widely planted red grapes in the world, thriving in hot, dry, windy
conditions where other varieties would shrivel. It buds early,
ripens late, and tolerates drought and poor soils, making it the
natural choice for the sun-baked south.

## Châteauneuf-du-Pape and the southern Rhône

Grenache reaches its most famous expression in Châteauneuf-du-Pape,
where it is the dominant grape in a blend that may legally include up
to thirteen varieties. On the appellation's famous galets roulés —
the large rounded stones that store the day's heat and release it at
night — old-vine Grenache produces wines of warmth, sweet red fruit,
garrigue herbs and considerable alcohol. Châteaus like Rayas (made
from pure Grenache on sandy soils) and Beaucastel are legends.

## Priorat: Grenache reborn

In the 1990s, a group of pioneers led by René Barbier and Álvaro
Palacios transformed Priorat, a remote, mountainous corner of
Catalonia, into one of Spain's most prestigious regions. On the
region's distinctive *llicorella* slate soils, ancient
Garnacha (and Cariñena) vines clinging to terraced hillsides produce
intense, mineral, concentrated reds. Palacios's L'Ermita, from a tiny
old-vine parcel, is among Spain's most expensive wines.

## Old vines in the Barossa

Grenache reached Australia in the 19th century, and the Barossa
Valley is now home to some of the oldest Grenache vines anywhere —
bush vines over a century old, surviving because they were never
pulled out during fashions for other grapes. These dry-grown old
vines produce wines of remarkable depth and perfume, and a new
generation of producers has made single-vineyard old-vine Grenache a
cause célèbre.

## GSM and the art of the blend

Grenache rarely works better than in partnership. The classic GSM
blend — Grenache, Syrah, Mourvèdre — pairs Grenache's sweet fruit and
alcohol with Syrah's structure and spice and Mourvèdre's tannin,
colour and savour. This trio defines the southern Rhône and has been
exported across the wine world. Grenache also makes superb rosé,
notably in Provence and Tavel, and is a key grape in fortified
Banyuls and Maury.

## The character of Grenache

Grenache gives a pale-to-medium red, often deceptively light in
colour, with aromas of strawberry, raspberry, white pepper, dried
herbs and, in warm sites, a kirsch-like sweetness. Tannins are usually
soft and acidity moderate, while alcohol can be high — old-vine
Châteauneuf and Priorat routinely exceed 15%. With age, fine
Grenache develops leather, tobacco and a savoury, almost Burgundian
complexity that has won it many converts.

## Tasting and pairing

Serve Grenache slightly below room temperature to keep its alcohol in
check and its red fruit fresh. It pairs beautifully with the cuisine
of its homelands — grilled lamb, ratatouille, paella, herb-roasted
meats and Mediterranean stews. The lighter, fresher styles suit
charcuterie and roast poultry; the powerful old-vine wines stand up
to rich game and hard cheese.
