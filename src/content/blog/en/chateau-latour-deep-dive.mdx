Château Latour stands at the southern tip of the Pauillac commune,
between Pichon-Lalande and Château Léoville-Las Cases of Saint-
Julien. The estate is one of the five 1855 First Growths and, by
common consent, the most consistent of them across difficult
vintages. The defining feature of Latour is the *Enclos*: a walled
47-hectare vineyard surrounding the famous tower, planted to
Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc, and Petit Verdot, that
provides the fruit for the Grand Vin.

## The Enclos and the Grand Vin

The Enclos is the heart of Latour. The soils — gravel over clay
and limestone — give exceptional drainage and, crucially, a deep
clay sub-stratum that supplies water in dry vintages. The
combination explains the estate's regularity: Latour suffers less
than its peers in difficult years and often makes the most
serious wine of the Médoc in vintages others struggle with (1961,
1975, 1990, and 2003 are textbook examples).

## The wines: three tiers

Latour produces three wines. The Grand Vin (Château Latour) is
the flagship, made only from the Enclos and only from the best
parcels of any given vintage. Les Forts de Latour, the second
wine introduced commercially in 1966, comes from younger vines
in the Enclos and from parcels just outside. The Pauillac de
Latour (third wine) handles younger vines and lesser parcels.
All three wines are made to a serious standard.

## The François Pinault era

The estate was sold by the Pearson Group to François Pinault's
Artémis holding company in 1993. Under Pinault the cellar was
rebuilt and the estate moved to biodynamic and organic farming
across much of the Enclos. Production levels have been disciplined;
the Grand Vin's share of total production has if anything
decreased to ensure quality.

## The withdrawal from en primeur

In 2012 Latour announced that it would no longer sell its wines
en primeur — the centuries-old Bordeaux system of selling young
wine in the spring after the harvest, two years before bottling.
Instead, the estate retains every vintage in its own cellars and
releases bottled wine when it considers the wine to be ready to
drink — typically eight to fifteen years after the harvest.

## The implications of late release

The shift is unique among the top Bordeaux estates. By holding
back stock, Latour eliminates the en primeur speculative market
that has historically distorted Bordeaux pricing, while
accepting the carrying cost of maturing several vintages on the
estate. Critically, the policy gives consumers wine that is
genuinely closer to its drinking window — Latour 2010 was
released in 2024, well into its window.

## The longevity

A first-class Latour from a good vintage routinely lasts five
decades. The 1961 remains one of the great wines of the postwar
era. The 1982 is at full maturity now. The 1990 is approaching
its drinking window. The 2009 and 2010 — both critically rated
in the high 90s — are projected for forty-plus years of
ageability.

## Comparison to its First Growth peers

Among the First Growths, Latour is generally considered the most
muscular and the most consistent. Lafite is more aromatic and
refined; Margaux more perfumed and graceful; Mouton more
flamboyant; Haut-Brion more cerebral. Latour's reputation rests
on what it can do in difficult vintages — and on the unrivalled
authority of the great years.

## Visiting Latour

Latour does not offer regular public tours; access is by prior
appointment and is most readily arranged through the wine trade.
The estate sits at the end of a road outside the Pauillac village
boundary, with the famous tower visible from the river-side. The
adjacent Pichon-Lalande, Pichon Comtesse, and other Pauillac and
Saint-Julien estates are easier to visit. Find Latour and
