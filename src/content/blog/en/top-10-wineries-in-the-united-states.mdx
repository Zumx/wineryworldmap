American fine wine was announced to the world on 24 May 1976 in Paris, when Steven Spurrier's blind tasting placed California wines — Stag's Leap Wine Cellars Cabernet Sauvignon and Chateau Montelena Chardonnay — above the best of Bordeaux and Burgundy in the judgement of a panel of French tasters. The event, known as the Judgement of Paris, was dismissed in France at the time but was the founding myth of the modern American wine industry. Since then, Napa Valley has produced some of the most discussed, collected, and expensive wines in the world, while Oregon's Willamette Valley, Sonoma's Russian River Valley, and Washington State's Walla Walla have developed distinct fine wine identities of their own.

## 1. Robert Mondavi Winery, Napa Valley

Robert Mondavi's departure from the family Charles Krug winery in 1966 to establish his own estate in Oakville was the pivotal act in the creation of modern Napa Valley as a fine wine region. Mondavi championed quality Cabernet Sauvignon, introduced barrel fermentation for Fumé Blanc (his Sauvignon Blanc label, a name he popularised), invested in research at UC Davis, and — crucially — connected Napa to the French wine world through collaboration with Baron Philippe de Rothschild in the Opus One joint venture. The Robert Mondavi Reserve Cabernet Sauvignon from the To Kalon vineyard in Oakville is the prestige flagship; the To Kalon Fumé Blanc Reserve is one of California's finest whites. Mondavi's death in 2008 did not diminish the estate's importance to American wine history.

## 2. Opus One, Oakville, Napa Valley

The joint venture between Robert Mondavi and Baron Philippe de Rothschild, conceived in 1978 and first produced in 1979, Opus One was the statement that Napa Valley was serious enough to partner with the owner of Mouton Rothschild. The wine — a Cabernet Sauvignon-dominant Bordeaux blend from the Oakville bench — is produced entirely from estate vineyards between Highway 29 and the Silverado Trail. The gravity-flow winery, designed by Scott Johnson and completed in 1991, is among the most architecturally ambitious in the world. The Opus One 2007, 2012, and 2018 are the contemporary benchmarks. Overture (a non-vintage blend from younger vine fruit) is the accessible second wine.

## 3. Stag's Leap Wine Cellars, Napa Valley

Warren Winiarski's Stag's Leap Wine Cellars in the Stags Leap District AVA won the red wine category at the 1976 Judgement of Paris with its 1973 SLV Cabernet Sauvignon — one of the most consequential moments in wine history. The SLV (Stag's Leap Vineyard) and Fay Vineyard Cabernet Sauvignons remain the estate's dual flagship single-vineyard expressions; Cask 23 (the prestige blend of SLV and Fay) is produced only in the finest years. The winery was sold to Antinori and Chateau Ste. Michelle in 2007 and subsequently managed by Chateau Ste. Michelle; the vineyards' quality has been maintained. The 1973, 1985, 1994, and 2002 Cask 23 are the historic benchmarks.

## 4. Screaming Eagle, Oakville, Napa Valley

Screaming Eagle is Napa Valley's defining cult wine: a Cabernet Sauvignon from a 57-acre estate in Oakville, produced in quantities of approximately 500 to 850 cases annually, available only to mailing list subscribers, and trading on the secondary market at prices comparable to first-growth Bordeaux. Jean Phillips established the estate in 1986 and made the first commercial vintage in 1992; the 1992 received a 99-point score from Robert Parker, launching the cult. Sold to Stan Kroenke (owner of Arsenal FC and various US sports franchises) in 2006, the estate has maintained exceptional quality under winemaker Nick Gislason. The Second Flight (from younger vine blocks) offers the estate's quality at marginally more accessible prices.

## 5. Harlan Estate, Oakville, Napa Valley

Bill Harlan's estate in the western hills above Oakville, established in 1984, produces a single Cabernet Sauvignon-dominant Bordeaux blend of legendary concentration and age-worthiness. The Harlan Estate wine — typically 70-80% Cabernet Sauvignon with Merlot, Cabernet Franc, and Petit Verdot — has received perfect or near-perfect scores in most vintages since the mid-1990s. The 1994, 1997, 2001, and 2013 are the canonical benchmarks. The Maiden (the estate's second wine, from younger vine blocks) is produced in larger quantities. Harlan's Bond project (five single-vineyard Cabernets from Napa estates) extends the same philosophy across the valley. Access is by mailing list only; no public tours.

## 6. Ridge Vineyards, Monte Bello, Santa Cruz Mountains

Ridge Monte Bello is the most historically significant and internationally respected Cabernet Sauvignon produced outside Napa Valley — a wine from the highest elevations of the Santa Cruz Mountains that placed second at the 1976 Judgement of Paris and then triumphed at the 30th-anniversary retasting in 2006. The Monte Bello (Cabernet Sauvignon-dominant with Merlot, Petit Verdot, and Cabernet Franc) is produced from steep, rocky limestone vineyards at 2,300 feet and is among the longest-lived wines produced in California. Winemaker Paul Draper, at Ridge from 1969 until his retirement in 2016, defined the estate's philosophy of minimal intervention and long barrel ageing. The Lytton Springs Zinfandel from Sonoma's Dry Creek Valley is the estate's most popular red.

## 7. Rochioli Vineyards, Russian River Valley, Sonoma

Joe Rochioli Jr.'s estate in the heart of the Russian River Valley's cool Green Valley sub-region produces what many consider the finest Pinot Noir in California and one of the finest in the world. The West Block Pinot Noir — from old vines on a west-facing river-terrace block — is the prestige flagship and is allocated to mailing list members in quantities so small that many subscribers receive only a few bottles. The East Block and River Block Pinot Noirs, the estate Chardonnay, and the Sauvignon Blanc (one of California's finest, from a family site planted in the 1960s) complete the range. Rochioli's bottlings are available only at the winery and through an oversubscribed mailing list; they are among the most sought-after California wines on the secondary market.

## 8. Domaine Drouhin Oregon, Willamette Valley

Véronique Drouhin-Boss, representing the fourth generation of the Drouhin family of Beaune, produces Oregon Pinot Noir from a single estate vineyard on the red volcanic soil of the Dundee Hills AVA with the same philosophy her family applies to Burgundy. The Domaine Drouhin Oregon Pinot Noir and Laurène (the estate's single-vineyard prestige expression) have consistently placed Oregon in serious international Pinot Noir conversation since the first vintage in 1988. The Arthur Chardonnay (planted specifically to demonstrate that Willamette Valley could produce Burgundian-scale white wine) and the Roserock (a single-vineyard project in the Eola-Amity Hills) extend the estate's range. Drouhin's investment in Oregon was itself a signal to the wine world that Willamette Valley Pinot Noir merited European-level attention.

## 9. Eyrie Vineyards, Willamette Valley, Oregon

David Lett planted the first Pinot Noir vines in the Willamette Valley in 1965, at a time when conventional wisdom held that vinifera could not ripen in Oregon. The 1975 Eyrie South Block Reserve Pinot Noir placed second at a 1979 Gault Millau tasting of world Pinot Noirs in Paris — a result that prompted Robert Drouhin of Maison Drouhin to challenge Eyrie to a rematch (which Eyrie again won narrowly), and subsequently to invest in his own Oregon estate. Eyrie's Pinot Noir and Pinot Gris (one of the first commercial US Pinot Gris plantings) remain benchmarks. Now managed by David's son Jason Lett, Eyrie continues as the historical and philosophical foundation of the Oregon wine industry.

## 10. Williams Selyem, Russian River Valley, Sonoma

Burt Williams and Ed Selyem began producing Pinot Noir in a garage in Guerneville in 1981, and over the following two decades built a cult following for wines that were foundational to the California Pinot Noir movement. The estate was sold to John Dyson in 1998 and has continued under winemaker Bob Cabral (until 2014) and Jeff Mangahas. The Williams Selyem Rochioli Riverblock Vineyard Pinot Noir, Precious Mountain Pinot Noir, and Westside Road Neighbors are the prestige vineyard-designate expressions. The Chardonnays — from Allen Vineyard and Heintz Vineyard in the Russian River Valley — are among California's finest. Williams Selyem remains mailing-list-only in allocations.

## Napa AVA vs Sonoma vs Oregon Willamette vs Washington Walla Walla

The four major American fine wine regions are distinct in climate, variety, and style. Napa Valley AVA — particularly Oakville, Rutherford, and the hillside sub-appellations — produces the world's most collected Cabernet Sauvignon: powerful, concentrated, structured, and capable of 30+ years of cellaring. Sonoma's Russian River Valley, cooled by Pacific fog that rolls in through the Petaluma Gap, is the finest California site for Pinot Noir and Chardonnay. Oregon's Willamette Valley, further north and significantly cooler than Napa, produces the most Burgundy-like Pinot Noir in the New World. Washington State's Walla Walla Valley, on the eastern Columbia Plateau, produces Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, and blends of dense fruit concentration in a continental climate unlike anything in California.
