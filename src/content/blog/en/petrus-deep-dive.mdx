Pomerol is the only major Bordeaux appellation without a formal
classification — no Premiers Crus, no 1855 ranking, no St-Emilion-style
hierarchy. And yet Pomerol contains Petrus, which since the late
20th century has consistently been the most expensive Bordeaux
wine at first release. Petrus has 11.5 hectares of vines, no
château worth photographing, and produces approximately 30,000
bottles a year. The signature is a wine almost entirely from
Merlot grown on a deposit of iron-rich blue clay — the
crasse de fer — that no other Pomerol estate fully owns.

## The estate

Petrus has been owned since 1961 by the Moueix family, the
négociant dynasty that also owns Trotanoy and several other
Pomerol estates and that long managed Cheval Blanc on the St-Emilion
plateau. Jean-Pierre Moueix took over after the death of Madame
Loubat, who had directed the estate for the previous three
decades; Jean-François Moueix and his son Edouard now run the
estate.

## The vineyard and soil

Petrus sits at the top of the Pomerol plateau, on a 40-metre
elevation that is the appellation's highest point. The unique
feature is the blue clay subsoil — a deep, dense, iron-rich clay
that holds water effectively and keeps the vines hydrated through
hot summers. The vineyard is 95% Merlot, 5% Cabernet Franc.

## Vinification and ageing

The harvest is done in a single morning, when possible, to ensure
optimum ripeness throughout. Fermentation takes place in concrete
vats; the wine is aged in 50% new oak for 18 to 22 months. The
estate is hand-bottled and not filtered.

## The reputation

Petrus came to international prominence in the 1940s and 1950s as
the favourite wine of the British Royal Family and certain old
American families. By the 1980s it was selling at prices that
exceeded any of the Médoc First Growths, and by the 2000s a single
bottle of a top vintage routinely fetched €4,000 to €6,000 at
release. The 1989 and 1990 vintages are considered Pomerol
benchmarks.

## Comparison with Trotanoy and Cheval Blanc

Trotanoy, Petrus's smaller and less famous neighbour also owned by
Moueix, offers a comparable style at a fraction of the price. Cheval
Blanc on the adjacent St-Emilion plateau (now LVMH-owned) was for
much of the 20th century Bordeaux's other "right bank" superstar —
a higher percentage of Cabernet Franc and a slightly more elegant
profile.

## Ageing potential

Top vintages of Petrus age effortlessly for 40 to 60 years. The
1947, 1961, 1982, 1989, 1990, 2000, 2005, 2010, and 2015 vintages
are particular reference points. The wines are softer and richer
in their first decade than the great Médocs, with classic Merlot
plum-and-truffle notes that develop with bottle age.

## Visiting

Petrus does not accept visitors. The estate has no formal
reception facilities and no cellar door. The vineyards are
visible from the public road south of the village of Pomerol;
neighbouring estates such as Vieux Château Certan, La Conseillante,
and L'Évangile are easier to visit and offer comparable Pomerol
experience at lower prices.
