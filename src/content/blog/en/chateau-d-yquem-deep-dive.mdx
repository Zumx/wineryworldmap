Château d'Yquem is the only estate classified as Premier Cru
Supérieur in the 1855 Bordeaux ranking — a category created
specifically for it, above the eleven Premier Cru estates of
Sauternes-Barsac. The wine has been made since the 16th century,
and continuously refined under the Lur-Saluces family from 1785
to 1999; since 1999 the property has been owned by LVMH. Yquem is
not merely the most famous sweet wine; it is one of the most
demanding wines in the world to make, requiring multiple
hand-harvests of botrytised grapes and a brutal level of
selection.

## The vineyard

Yquem covers 113 hectares on a low, sandy-gravel hill above the
Ciron, a small left-bank tributary of the Garonne. The Ciron's
cold water, meeting the warmer Garonne, generates autumn fogs
that condense on the grapes and bring the noble rot — Botrytis
cinerea — which concentrates sugar and develops the apricot,
honey, and pineapple aromatics that define great Sauternes. The
vineyard is planted 80% Sémillon and 20% Sauvignon Blanc.

## Noble rot and the harvest

The harvest at Yquem can last six to eight weeks, with pickers
returning to the same vines four to ten times. Each pass removes
only the grapes that have reached the optimum botrytis
concentration; under-affected and over-affected grapes are left
or discarded. In dry years (with insufficient botrytis) no grand
vin is produced at all. The estate famously skipped the 1972,
1974, 1992, and 2012 vintages.

## Vinification

Fermentation takes place in oak barrels, after a long pneumatic
press. The wine stops naturally when alcohol kills the yeast,
typically around 13.5–14% alcohol with 120–150 g/l of residual
sugar. The wine then ages for 30 to 36 months in new oak before
bottling — a regime that would crush almost any other wine but
that Yquem's concentration easily absorbs.

## The Y (Ygrec)

Since the 1950s the estate has produced a dry white called Y —
typically 75% Sauvignon Blanc and 25% Sémillon, from less
botrytised grapes. Initially only declared in years that did not
warrant a grand vin, Y has been a regular release since 2004 and
is now one of the most respected dry whites in Bordeaux.

## Ageing potential

Yquem ages effectively forever. The 1811 (the famous "Comet
vintage") still appears at auctions in drinkable condition. The
1900 and 1921 vintages are touchstones. Most modern vintages will
develop for 50 to 100 years; a great vintage is at its peak
between 30 and 60 years after release.

## Buying and drinking

A bottle of current-release Yquem costs €400-500; older great
vintages can reach four-digit prices per bottle. The wine is
traditionally served with foie gras, blue cheese, fruit-based
desserts, or simply on its own as the meal closes. A 750ml
bottle, served in 50ml portions, can serve fifteen guests at a
formal dinner without diminishing the experience.

## Visiting

Yquem accepts visits by appointment for the trade; consumer
visits are extremely limited. The château itself sits prominently
above the Sauternes village and can be photographed from the
public road. The neighbouring villages of Sauternes, Bommes, and
Fargues are an easy day-trip from Bordeaux.
