Malbec is the great immigrant success story of the wine world. A
minor blending grape in its French homeland, it crossed the Atlantic
in the 19th century and became, in the high deserts of Argentina, one
of the most recognisable red wines on the planet. Inky, plush and
generous, modern Malbec is Argentina's flagship — but the grape's
older, sterner self still lives in the black wines of Cahors.

## French origins

Malbec is native to south-west France, where it goes by several
names — Côt, Auxerrois, and most famously Malbec. Its historic home
is Cahors, on the Lot river, where it must by law make up at least
70% of the appellation's wine. Cahors Malbec is a different beast
from its Argentine cousin: darker, more tannic and savoury, with
notes of black plum, iron and tobacco, built on limestone and
gravel terraces. It was once known as "the black wine of Cahors" and
prized across medieval Europe.

## A bit player in Bordeaux

Malbec was historically one of the permitted grapes of Bordeaux,
where it added colour and flesh to blends. But it is sensitive to
frost and coulure (poor fruit set), and the catastrophic frost of
1956 destroyed much of the Bordeaux planting. Growers replanted with
hardier Merlot and Cabernet, and Malbec dwindled to a footnote on the
Left Bank. Its future lay elsewhere.

## The journey to Argentina

Malbec arrived in Argentina in the 1850s, brought by the French
agronomist Michel Pouget at the request of the provincial government
of Mendoza. In the dry, sunny, high-altitude conditions of the Andes
foothills, the grape thrived as it never had in France — ripening
fully, building rich colour and softer tannins. For over a century it
was a workhorse of bulk wine; the quality revolution came only in the
1990s.

## Why altitude matters

The secret of fine Argentine Malbec is elevation. Mendoza's best
vineyards sit between 900 and 1,500 metres above sea level, with
some in the Uco Valley climbing higher still. At altitude, intense
daytime sun ripens the fruit fully while cold nights preserve
acidity and freshness, and the strong ultraviolet light thickens the
skins, concentrating colour and tannin. The result is a wine of both
power and lift — ripe black fruit with a cool, floral, violet-scented
edge.

## The regions of Argentine Malbec

Within Mendoza, distinct zones give distinct styles. Luján de Cuyo,
the historic heart, produces structured, classic Malbec from old
vines around Vistalba and Las Compuertas. The high Uco Valley —
Tupungato, Gualtallary, San Carlos — is the modern frontier, giving
fresher, more mineral and age-worthy wines from stony, calcareous
soils. Beyond Mendoza, Salta's Calchaquí Valley grows Malbec at
extreme altitudes above 1,700 metres, and Patagonia offers a cooler,
more elegant expression.

## The producers who led the revolution

Catena Zapata, under Nicolás Catena and his daughter Laura, drove the
high-altitude quality movement and proved Argentine Malbec could
rival the world's best. Other key names include Achával-Ferrer, Viña
Cobos (founded by Paul Hobbs), Zuccardi (whose Concreto and Finca
Piedra Infinita explore the Uco Valley's terroirs), Bodega Noemía in
Patagonia, and the French-owned Cheval des Andes, a joint venture of
Cheval Blanc.

## Tasting and pairing Malbec

Argentine Malbec is generous and approachable: deep purple, with
aromas of blackberry, plum, violet and, with oak, vanilla and cocoa.
The tannins are ripe and the texture plush. It is the classic match
for grilled red meat — Argentina's asado is its natural partner — and
the higher-altitude, fresher styles also pair well with roasted
vegetables and hard cheeses. Cahors, by contrast, wants slow-cooked,
rustic dishes like cassoulet and duck confit.
