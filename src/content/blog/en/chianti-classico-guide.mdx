Chianti Classico is the historic heartland of Tuscan wine, the
cluster of hills between Florence and Siena where Sangiovese has been
grown since the Middle Ages. For decades the name was dragged down by
the cheap, straw-flask Chianti of the wider region, but Classico — a
separate, stricter denomination marked by the Gallo Nero, the black
rooster — has reclaimed its place among Italy's great reds. This is a
guide to the grape, the rules and the estates.

## Sangiovese, the soul of Chianti

Chianti Classico is built on Sangiovese, a grape of high acidity,
firm tannin and savoury red-cherry, herb and tobacco character.
Italian law requires a minimum of 80% Sangiovese; many of the best
estates now use 100%. The remainder may be local grapes like
Canaiolo and Colorino, or — controversially — international varieties
such as Cabernet and Merlot, though the trend is decisively back
toward purity.

## The Gallo Nero and the boundaries

The black rooster (Gallo Nero) on the neck of every bottle is the
seal of the Consorzio Chianti Classico and marks wine from the
historic zone only — roughly the communes of Greve, Radda,
Gaiole, Castellina, Castelnuovo Berardenga and parts of others.
This is distinct from the much larger Chianti DOCG that surrounds it.
The Classico zone sits higher and cooler, on galestro and alberese
soils that give structure and longevity.

## The three tiers

Chianti Classico has a three-level quality pyramid. **Annata**, the
base, is aged at least twelve months. **Riserva** is aged at least
twenty-four months, including three in bottle, and shows more depth.
**Gran Selezione**, introduced in 2014, sits at the top: made only
from a single estate's own grapes, aged at least thirty months, and
since 2021 increasingly tied to named geographic units. It is the
denomination's answer to its finest wines.

## The new geographic units (UGA)

In 2021 Chianti Classico introduced eleven Unità Geografiche
Aggiuntive — additional geographic units — that may appear on Gran
Selezione labels: San Casciano, Montefioralle, Panzano, Greve,
Lamole, Radda, Gaiole, Castellina, Vagliagli, Castelnuovo Berardenga
and San Donato in Poggio. This Burgundian step toward naming distinct
terroirs is reshaping how the region presents its top wines.

## The great estates

The hills are dense with historic and modern names. Castello di
Brolio (Barone Ricasoli), where Bettino Ricasoli formalised the
Chianti recipe in the 19th century, is the oldest. Castello di Ama in
Gaiole, Fontodi in Panzano (whose Flaccianello is a benchmark),
Felsina in Castelnuovo Berardenga, Isole e Olena, Monteverro and
Querciabella all make reference wines. Antinori, the great Florentine
house, anchors the zone with its dramatic Bargino winery and the
Tignanello estate nearby.

## Chianti Classico and the Super Tuscans

The Super Tuscan movement of the 1970s was born partly in frustration
with Chianti's old rules. Estates like Antinori bottled Sangiovese
and Cabernet blends as humble *vino da tavola* rather than meet the
white-grape requirements then in force — and those wines, Tignanello
and Sassicaia among them, became world-famous. The rules have since
caught up, and much of that quality now flows back into Chianti
Classico itself, especially at Gran Selezione level.

## Visiting the Chianti hills

The Chiantigiana road (SR222) winds through the heart of the zone
from Florence to Siena, past Greve, Panzano, Radda and Gaiole — one
of Italy's most beautiful wine drives. Hilltop villages, medieval
castles turned wineries, and cypress-lined lanes make this prime
wine-tourism country. Most estates welcome visitors by appointment,
and Greve's central piazza is a fine base for tasting across the
denomination.
