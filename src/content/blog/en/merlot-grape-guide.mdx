Merlot is the grape that the wine world loves to underrate. Soft,
generous and easy to like, it has been dismissed as a crowd-pleaser
ever since a famous film line skewered it in 2004 — yet the single
most expensive red wine on Earth, Petrus, is made almost entirely
from Merlot. The truth is that Merlot ranges from inexpensive
everyday red to one of the most profound wines ever made, and the
difference is all about where it grows.

## A grape of the Right Bank

Merlot is native to Bordeaux, where it is the most widely planted
grape and the dominant variety of the Right Bank — Saint-Émilion,
Pomerol and their satellites. It ripens earlier than Cabernet
Sauvignon, which makes it well suited to the cooler, clay-rich soils
of the Right Bank, where Cabernet struggles. The name derives from
*merle*, the blackbird, said to favour its early-ripening sweet fruit.

## Why Pomerol made it legendary

Pomerol, a small appellation with no formal classification, is the
spiritual home of great Merlot. On its bed of clay and iron-rich
"crasse de fer", Merlot produces wines of extraordinary richness,
velvet texture and depth. Petrus, made from almost pure Merlot on a
button of blue clay, is the icon; Le Pin, Lafleur, L'Église-Clinet
and Vieux Château Certan are among the other legends. These are
wines that prove Merlot's potential for greatness beyond any doubt.

## Merlot in the blend

Across most of the world, Merlot's primary role is in the blend. It
softens and fleshes out Cabernet Sauvignon, filling the mid-palate
and rounding the tannins. The classic Bordeaux blend — Cabernet for
structure and longevity, Merlot for flesh and approachability — is
imitated everywhere from Tuscany to the Napa Valley. Even on the
Cabernet-dominated Left Bank, Merlot is almost always a supporting
player.

## The grape's character

Merlot gives a deep ruby wine with aromas of plum, black cherry,
blackberry and, with age, fig, chocolate and cedar. It is generally
softer in tannin and lower in acidity than Cabernet, with a plush,
rounded texture that makes it approachable young. In cooler sites it
shows a more structured, herbal, savoury profile; in warmer sites it
becomes richer and fruitier, sometimes jammy when overripe.

## Merlot around the world

Beyond Bordeaux, Merlot is grown almost everywhere wine is made. Italy
uses it in Super Tuscans (Masseto, from Bolgheri, is a celebrated pure
Merlot) and across the north-east; Washington State makes serious,
structured Merlot; California produces both bulk and ambitious
bottlings; Chile has vast plantings, where it was historically
confused with Carmenère. Switzerland's Ticino has made Merlot its
signature, and the grape thrives in cooler corners worldwide.

## The Sideways effect and the recovery

The 2004 film *Sideways*, in which the lead character disparages
Merlot, triggered a measurable slump in American Merlot sales and a
surge for Pinot Noir. The episode became a famous case study in how
culture moves markets. But the grape has recovered, helped by serious
producers reminding drinkers that the world's greatest Right Bank
wines are, after all, Merlot.

## Tasting and pairing

Merlot's approachability makes it an ideal introduction to fine red
wine, but the best examples reward cellaring for a decade or more.
Its soft tannins and plush fruit pair with a wide range of food —
roast lamb and beef, duck, mushroom dishes, and medium-hard cheeses.
The richer New World styles suit grilled meats; the structured Right
Bank wines shine alongside classic French cuisine.
