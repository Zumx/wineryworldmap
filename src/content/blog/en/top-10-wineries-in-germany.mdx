Germany makes the most precise white wine on Earth, and almost all of
it is Riesling. From the slate ravines of the Mosel to the gentler
loam of the Pfalz, the country's best estates work cool-climate
vineyards that ripen slowly, hold acidity, and translate site into
the glass with almost forensic clarity. The classification system —
VDP's vineyard pyramid topped by Grosse Lage — now rivals Burgundy
for parcel-by-parcel detail. The ten producers below are the
benchmarks.

## 1. Egon Müller, Scharzhofberg (Saar)

The most famous name in German wine, and arguably the world's
greatest sweet Riesling. The Scharzhofberg, a steep slate slope in
the Saar valley, gives Egon Müller's Spätlese, Auslese and
botrytised Trockenbeerenauslese a tension and longevity that no
other estate matches. Bottles from great years — 1976, 1990, 2003 —
are auctioned for record prices.

## 2. Joh. Jos. Prüm, Wehlen (Mosel)

Founded in its modern form in 1911, J.J. Prüm is the reference for
the classic fruity-sweet Mosel style. The Wehlener Sonnenuhr, marked
by its hillside sundial, produces Rieslings of weightless intensity
that often need a decade to shed their youthful reduction and reveal
their true delicacy.

## 3. Weingut Robert Weil, Kiedrich (Rheingau)

The Rheingau's leading estate, owned since 1988 by the Suntory group
but run with uncompromising quality. The Kiedricher Gräfenberg
Grosses Gewächs (dry) and its botrytis wines up to Eiswein and TBA
set the regional standard. Founded in 1875, it survived two world
wars to become a model of consistency.

## 4. Weingut Dönnhoff, Oberhausen (Nahe)

Helmut Dönnhoff turned the Nahe into a fine-wine region in
international eyes. The Niederhäuser Hermannshöhle and the
volcanic-soiled Oberhäuser Brücke yield dry Grosse Gewächse of
crystalline minerality and sweet wines of extraordinary purity. Son
Cornelius now leads.

## 5. Weingut Keller, Flörsheim-Dalsheim (Rheinhessen)

Klaus-Peter Keller made Rheinhessen — once a bulk-wine flatland — a
source of cult dry Riesling. His G-Max, from an undisclosed old-vine
parcel, is among the most sought-after white wines in the world,
trading far above first-growth Bordeaux at release.

## 6. Weingut Wittmann, Westhofen (Rheinhessen)

A biodynamic estate and the other pole of the Rheinhessen revival.
The Morstein and Aulerde Grosse Gewächse are dense, mineral,
age-worthy dry Rieslings from the limestone-rich Wonnegau.

## 7. Dr. Loosen, Bernkastel (Mosel)

Ernst Loosen restored a portfolio of grand-cru Mosel sites —
Wehlener Sonnenuhr, Erdener Prälat, Ürziger Würzgarten — and
became Germany's great ambassador abroad. The estate works old,
ungrafted vines on pure slate without herbicides.

## 8. Weingut Fritz Haag, Brauneberg (Mosel)

The master of the Brauneberger Juffer-Sonnenuhr, one of the Middle
Mosel's warmest slate slopes. Fritz Haag's Rieslings span racy
Kabinett to honeyed Auslese, all marked by a signature precision and
a long, salty finish.

## 9. Weingut Müller-Catoir, Haardt (Pfalz)

A historic Pfalz estate at the foot of the Haardt mountains, known
for powerful, aromatic dry Rieslings and remarkable work with
Rieslaner and Scheurebe. The Breumel in den Mauern and Bürgergarten
parcels are flagships.

## 10. Weingut Bürklin-Wolf, Wachenheim (Pfalz)

One of the Pfalz's largest fine estates, biodynamic since the early
2000s, working a string of grand-cru sites along the
Mittelhaardt — Kirchenstück, Jesuitengarten, Pechstein. Its dry
Grosse Gewächse show the riper, broader Pfalz expression of
Riesling.

## German wine classification

The most useful key to a German label is the ripeness/style
hierarchy. Kabinett, Spätlese and Auslese denote increasing grape
ripeness and, traditionally, sweetness; Beerenauslese, Eiswein and
Trockenbeerenauslese are rare sweet wines. The word *trocken* means
dry. The VDP, the association of top estates, overlays a
site-based pyramid: Gutswein, Ortswein, Erste Lage and, at the
summit, Grosse Lage — whose dry wines are labelled Grosses
Gewächs (GG). Learning these two systems unlocks almost every
serious German bottle.

## Visiting the German wine regions

The Mosel is the most scenic, its vineyards rising at angles up to
65 degrees above the river between Trier and Koblenz. Bernkastel-Kues
and Traben-Trarbach make good bases. The Rheingau runs along the
Rhine near Wiesbaden; the Pfalz, warmer and sunnier, centres on the
Deutsche Weinstrasse around Deidesheim and Forst. Most estates take
appointments, and the cooler, low-alcohol style of German Riesling
makes for unusually civilised all-day tasting.
