New Zealand's wine story is one of the fastest transformations in modern wine history. In 1973, there were fewer than a hundred hectares of vinifera vines in the country. Today there are over 40,000 hectares, producing wines that have won the world's most prestigious blind tastings in Sauvignon Blanc and, increasingly, in Pinot Noir. The success of Marlborough in creating a globally recognised style of Sauvignon Blanc — grass, capsicum, passionfruit, and grapefruit in a piercing dry white — was the initial engine, but Pinot Noir from Central Otago, Chardonnay from Hawke's Bay, and Syrah from the Gimblett Gravels have all added depth to a country that is far more than one variety.

## 1. Cloudy Bay, Marlborough

Cloudy Bay's 1985 Sauvignon Blanc, produced by David Hohnen and winemaker Kevin Judd, effectively defined Marlborough as a wine region and established New Zealand as a serious wine country in one vintage. The pungent, expressive, herbaceous and tropical-fruited style — bone dry, high acid, with an intensity no European Sauvignon Blanc had previously achieved — was unlike anything the market had tasted and became the template for a generation of New Zealand Sauvignon Blanc. Now owned by LVMH's Möet Hennessy, Cloudy Bay produces a complex range including Te Koko (Sauvignon Blanc fermented and aged in oak), Pelorus (méthode traditionnelle sparkling), Pinot Noir, and the Chardonnay. The standard Sauvignon Blanc remains the world's most recognised New Zealand wine.

## 2. Dog Point Vineyard, Marlborough

Ivan Sutherland and James Healy left Cloudy Bay (where they had been viticulturist and chief winemaker respectively) to establish Dog Point in 2002, farming their own Brancott Valley vineyards. The Dog Point Sauvignon Blanc Section 94 — fermented with wild yeasts, barrel-fermented and aged in old oak without temperature control — is one of the most complex Sauvignon Blancs in the world, moving well beyond the typical Marlborough style toward something textured, mineral, and profoundly ageworthy. The standard Sauvignon Blanc and the Pinot Noir are reliable and excellent at their respective price levels. Dog Point represents the alternative direction for Marlborough — away from technical freshness and toward genuine natural complexity.

## 3. Greywacke, Marlborough

Kevin Judd, the original winemaker at Cloudy Bay who did more than any individual to establish the visual and sensory identity of Marlborough Sauvignon Blanc through 25 harvests at Cloudy Bay, left to create his own estate in 2009. Greywacke (named for the greywacke stone that dominates Marlborough's geology) produces the Wild Sauvignon — a wild-yeast, barrel-fermented Sauvignon Blanc of complexity and texture comparable to Dog Point's Section 94 — alongside the more conventionally styled Sauvignon Blanc and a serious Pinot Noir from the Southern Valleys. The Pinot Gris and Riesling complete a small range of exceptional quality.

## 4. Felton Road, Central Otago

Nigel Greening's biodynamic estate in the Bannockburn sub-region of Central Otago is the reference point for Central Otago Pinot Noir and, many argue, for New Zealand Pinot Noir overall. The Felton Road Block 3 and Block 5 Pinot Noirs — single-block expressions from contrasting soils (schist and silt respectively) within the estate — are the flagship wines, capable of ageing for fifteen or more years. The Cornish Point Pinot Noir, from a separate site across the Cromwell Basin, adds a third terroir expression. Felton Road's Chardonnay and Riesling are equally serious; the organic/biodynamic farming (certified since 2010) has become a model for the region. The 2012 and 2015 Block 3 are the modern benchmark vintages.

## 5. Rippon Vineyard, Wanaka

Rippon's lakeside setting above Lake Wanaka is one of the most photographed vineyards in the world, and the wines match the scenery. The Mature Vine Pinot Noir and Emma's Block Pinot Noir are the flagships, from vines planted by Rolfe Mills in the early 1980s — among the oldest in Central Otago. The biodynamic farming (the estate has been biodynamic for decades) contributes to a complexity and earthiness in the wines that distinguishes them from the more fruit-forward Central Otago mainstream. The Rippon Gamay and the Osteospermum (a Riesling) are idiosyncratic expressions of a producer that has always followed its own path. Mills Reef, the family's secondary label, offers more accessible pricing from the same vineyard philosophy.

## 6. Te Mata Estate, Hawke's Bay

Te Mata, established in 1895 and one of New Zealand's oldest continuously operating wineries, makes Coleraine — a Cabernet Sauvignon-Merlot-Cabernet Franc blend from the Havelock North hillside — that is the most celebrated Bordeaux-variety red produced in New Zealand. First made in the 1982 vintage, the Coleraine has established Hawke's Bay's Tuki Tuki Valley as the finest site in New Zealand for Cabernet-based reds. The Bullnose Syrah, from the Gimblett Gravels (a stony, free-draining sub-appellation within Hawke's Bay), has become an important reference for New Zealand Syrah. The Elston Chardonnay is among Hawke's Bay's finest whites.

## 7. Craggy Range, Hawke's Bay

Terry Peabody's Craggy Range estate, established in 1998 and based at Havelock North, spans three regions — Hawke's Bay, Martinborough, and Marlborough — but its finest wines come from the Te Muna Road Vineyard in Martinborough (Pinot Noir and Sauvignon Blanc) and the Gimblett Gravels (Syrah and Cabernet Sauvignon in Hawke's Bay). The Sophia Merlot-Cabernet Franc-Cabernet Sauvignon blend and the Sophia Syrah (both from the Gimblett) are the prestige reds; the Te Muna Pinot Noir demonstrates Martinborough's capacity for elegant, restrained Pinot at the highest level. Craggy Range's winery and restaurant at Havelock North is one of the finest food-and-wine destination experiences in New Zealand.

## 8. Trinity Hill, Hawke's Bay

John Hancock's Trinity Hill estate, based in the Gimblett Gravels, is one of Hawke's Bay's most respected producers of Syrah and Bordeaux varieties. The Homage Syrah — from the oldest Syrah vines in the Gimblett, planted in 1993 — is the prestige expression and one of New Zealand's finest Syrahs: dark, smoky, spicy, with the pepper and olive character that the stony, warm gravel site produces. The The Gimblett (a Cabernet Sauvignon-Merlot-Cabernet Franc blend from the same gravels) is the Bordeaux flagship. Trinity Hill's partnership with Hawke's Bay's Tempranillo and Montepulciano experimental plantings represents a forward-looking exploration of warm-climate varieties in the Gimblett.

## 9. Kumeu River, Auckland

Michael Brajkovich MW produces Burgundy-style Chardonnay from his family estate in Kumeu, west of Auckland, at a level that consistently outperforms European Chardonnay at comparable prices. The Mate's Vineyard Chardonnay — single parcel, barrel-fermented with wild yeasts, aged on lees in French oak — regularly appears on international best-Chardonnay lists and has been compared favourably to Premier Cru Burgundy in blind tastings. The Kumeu River Hunting Hill Chardonnay is the second single-vineyard expression; the village-level Chardonnay is exceptional value. Brajkovich became New Zealand's first Master of Wine in 1989, and his approach to Chardonnay has influenced producers across the country.

## 10. Mt Difficulty, Central Otago

Mt Difficulty's Bannockburn estate, farmed on the steeply terraced schist slopes above the Cromwell Basin, has been producing benchmark Central Otago Pinot Noir since 1992. The Single Vineyard range — Pipeclay Terrace Pinot Noir, Mansons Farm Pinot Noir, and Musings Pinot Noir — explores distinct parcels within the Bannockburn sub-region, while the Roaring Meg Pinot Noir (named for a local tributary) is the accessible, early-drinking expression. The Long Gully Pinot Gris and the Bannockburn Pinot Blanc are among the more serious white wine expressions in Central Otago. Mt Difficulty's viewing restaurant above the vineyard is one of the most spectacular winery dining experiences in the South Island.

## Marlborough vs Central Otago vs Hawke's Bay

The three major quality regions produce fundamentally different wines. Marlborough, on the northern tip of the South Island, has a cool, maritime-influenced climate perfectly suited to Sauvignon Blanc (85% of plantings) and increasingly impressive Pinot Noir and Chardonnay in the Southern Valleys. Central Otago, the world's southernmost wine region at 45°S, is a continental high-altitude desert producing intensely structured Pinot Noir of growing international importance. Hawke's Bay, on the North Island's east coast, has a warmer, drier climate that allows Bordeaux varieties and Syrah to ripen fully in the Gimblett Gravels, while still producing elegant Chardonnay in cooler inland sites.
