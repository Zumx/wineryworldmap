Port is the fortified wine of Portugal's Douro Valley, made by
arresting fermentation with grape spirit so that the wine keeps its
natural sweetness and gains strength. It is one of the oldest
demarcated wine regions in the world — the Douro was defined in 1756
— and one of the most dramatic, its terraced vineyards clinging to
schist slopes above a river gorge. Understanding Port means
understanding how a handful of styles diverge from the same starting
point.

## How Port is made

Port begins as a red wine fermented from indigenous Douro grapes —
Touriga Nacional, Touriga Franca, Tinta Roriz, Tinta Barroca and
Tinto Cão chief among them. Partway through fermentation, when about
half the sugar remains, the must is fortified with a clear grape
spirit called aguardente, raising the alcohol to around 19–20% and
killing the yeast. The result is a sweet, powerful wine that is then
aged in one of two fundamentally different ways.

## The two paths: bottle versus barrel

Every Port style descends from a single choice: whether to age the
wine in the reductive environment of a sealed bottle, or the
oxidative environment of a wooden cask. Bottle-aged Ports —
Vintage and Late Bottled Vintage — keep their deep colour and fresh
fruit and develop slowly. Cask-aged Ports — tawnies — gradually pale
to amber, lose fruit and gain nutty, caramel and dried-fig
complexity through controlled exposure to air. This single fork
explains the entire Port family.

## Vintage Port

The pinnacle. Vintage Port is made only in "declared" years — when
shippers judge the harvest exceptional, typically three or four
times a decade — from the best vineyards of a single vintage. It is
aged in cask for around two years, then bottled unfiltered to mature
for decades in the bottle, throwing a heavy sediment that requires
decanting. Declared years such as 1963, 1977, 1994, 2011 and 2016
are legendary.

## Tawny Port

Tawny Port is aged in cask until it turns amber and develops its
characteristic oxidative profile. The finest carry an indication of
average age — 10, 20, 30 or 40 Years — blended to a house style.
Colheita is a tawny from a single year, cask-aged for at least seven.
These are the most versatile Ports, served lightly chilled, and
unlike Vintage they are ready to drink on release and stable once
opened.

## Ruby, Reserve and LBV

The everyday styles. Ruby Port is young, fruity and aged briefly;
Reserve Ruby is a step up. Late Bottled Vintage (LBV) is from a
single year, cask-aged four to six years, then bottled — a more
affordable approximation of Vintage character, usually filtered and
ready to drink. Crusted Port, a blend of several years bottled
unfiltered, throws a sediment like Vintage and is a connoisseur's
value.

## White and Rosé Port

White Port is made from white Douro grapes in dry to sweet styles,
and is the basis of the Porto tonic — white Port over ice with tonic
water and a twist of lemon, an aperitif beloved across northern
Portugal. Pink or Rosé Port is a modern, lightly fortified style for
chilled summer drinking. Both are minor in volume but increasingly
visible.

## The great shippers

The Port trade was built by English and Scottish merchant houses
established from the seventeenth century, and many names endure.
Taylor's, Fonseca and Croft (part of the Fladgate Partnership);
Graham's, Dow's, Warre's and Cockburn's (part of the Symington
family group); Quinta do Noval, famous for its tiny Nacional bottling
from ungrafted vines; and Niepoort, the great independent. The
lodges across the river in Vila Nova de Gaia, where the wine
traditionally aged in the cooler coastal air, are open to visitors.

## How to read a Port label

The key word is the style — Vintage, LBV, Tawny, Ruby, White — and,
for tawnies, the indication of age. A single-quinta Vintage names the
specific estate (Quinta do Vesúvio, Quinta dos Malvedos) and is
usually made in years not declared as full Vintage. The shipper's
name carries the house style. Sweetness and strength are givens; the
ageing method is everything.
