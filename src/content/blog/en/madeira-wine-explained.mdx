Madeira is the most extraordinary wine in the world to taste, and the
strangest to make. Produced on a steep volcanic island 600 kilometres
off the coast of Morocco, it is deliberately heated and oxidised — a
process that would ruin any other wine — to produce something almost
indestructible. An opened bottle keeps for months; a century-old
Madeira can taste astonishingly fresh. It is a wine that defies every
rule of cellaring.

## A wine born of accident

Madeira's character was discovered by accident in the age of sail.
The island was a provisioning stop for ships rounding Africa, and
casks of its wine, fortified to survive the voyage, crossed the
tropics in the holds of East Indiamen. The heat and motion
transformed the wine — and the transformed wine tasted better than
the original. Producers began to imitate the voyage on land, and the
modern styles of heating were born.

## Estufagem and canteiro

Two methods recreate that tropical heating. *Estufagem* uses heated
stainless-steel tanks (estufas) that hold the wine at around 45–50°C
for at least three months — the method for everyday Madeira.
*Canteiro*, reserved for the finest wines, ages the casks naturally
in the warm upper lofts of the lodges, where the heat of the
Madeiran sun slowly cooks the wine over years or decades. Canteiro
Madeira is among the most concentrated and long-lived wine on Earth.

## The noble grapes and their styles

Traditional Madeira is named by grape, and each grape corresponds to
a level of sweetness, from driest to sweetest:

- **Sercial** — the driest, grown highest and coolest; tangy, almond
  and lemon, an apéritif.
- **Verdelho** — off-dry, smoky and nutty, a versatile style.
- **Bual (Boal)** — medium-sweet, rich with raisin and coffee.
- **Malmsey (Malvasia)** — the sweetest, dark and luscious, all
  caramel, fig and toffee, yet always cut by Madeira's searing
  acidity.

A fifth grape, **Tinta Negra**, the island's workhorse, makes most
inexpensive Madeira and increasingly fine wines too.

## Reading age and labels

Madeira labels carry indications of age — 5, 10, 15, 20, 30 or 40
Years — denoting a blended average. *Colheita* (or Harvest) is from a
single year, aged at least five. *Frasqueira* or Vintage Madeira is
the summit: from a single year, canteiro-aged in cask for a minimum
of twenty years before bottling, and capable of lasting more than a
century. Acidity, not sugar, is what preserves it.

## The great producers

The Madeira trade is concentrated in a handful of historic firms in
Funchal, the island capital. The Madeira Wine Company houses the
famous Blandy's, Cossart Gordon, Leacock's and Miles brands; Blandy's
remains family-led after seven generations. Henriques & Henriques is
a major independent with extensive vineyard holdings; Barbeito, known
for elegant, lower-sugar canteiro wines, and Pereira d'Oliveira, with
a legendary cellar of ancient vintages, complete the front rank.

## Tasting and serving

Madeira is the most forgiving fine wine to serve: it needs no
decanting, suffers no harm from air, and an open bottle is stable for
months. Dry Sercial and Verdelho are served lightly chilled as
apéritifs; sweet Bual and Malmsey are classic with cheese, nuts and
dark desserts. A great Frasqueira, sipped slowly at room temperature,
delivers an intensity and length few wines of any kind can equal.

## Visiting Madeira

Funchal's lodges, several in the historic centre, offer tastings that
can span wines a century apart in age — the Blandy's Wine Lodge and
Pereira d'Oliveira's cellar are the classic visits. The vineyards
themselves cling to terraces (*poios*) on impossibly steep slopes,
worked by hand, with Sercial planted highest and the sweeter grapes
lower and warmer. The island's mild Atlantic climate makes it a
year-round destination.
