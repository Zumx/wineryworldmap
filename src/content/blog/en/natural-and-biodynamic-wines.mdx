Natural and biodynamic wine occupy overlapping but distinct philosophical territories. Biodynamic viticulture is a codified agricultural system with certification standards; natural wine is a loosely defined movement with no single governing body. Both have reshaped how a generation of winemakers thinks about the vineyard and the cellar, and both have attracted devoted advocates and equally vocal sceptics. Understanding the difference — and the best producers in each tradition — is increasingly important for anyone navigating the modern wine world.

## What Biodynamic Viticulture Actually Is

Biodynamic agriculture derives from a series of lectures given by Rudolf Steiner in 1924, published as the Agriculture Course. Steiner's cosmological framework treats the farm as a self-contained living organism, shaped by lunar cycles, planetary influences, and the living quality of the soil. The biodynamic calendar divides days into four categories — Root, Flower, Fruit, and Leaf — corresponding to the four classical elements and influencing when pruning, harvesting, and tasting are considered optimal. Whether or not the astronomical underpinning is taken literally, the practical outcome — extreme attention to soil biology, composting, and the avoidance of synthetic inputs — often produces measurable improvements in vine health and soil microbial life.

The Demeter Association, founded in Germany in 1928, is the principal international certification body for biodynamic producers. Demeter certification requires compliance with the full biodynamic standards including the use of specific preparations — BD 500 (cow manure fermented in a buried horn over winter, used as a soil spray) and BD 501 (finely ground quartz crystal packed in a horn over summer, sprayed on leaves) — and prohibits synthetic herbicides, pesticides, and chemical fertilisers. Biodyvin is the wine-specific biodynamic certification based in France, used by many of the Burgundian and Loire estates.

## Key Biodynamic Estates

Domaine Leflaive in Puligny-Montrachet converted to biodynamics under Anne-Claude Leflaive beginning in 1990, becoming one of the first and most celebrated biodynamic estates in Burgundy. The domaine's Puligny-Montrachet Premier Crus — Pucelles, Folatières, Clavoillon — and its Chevalier-Montrachet Grand Cru are benchmarks of white Burgundy, and the biodynamic conversion is widely credited with restoring the wines' complexity after a difficult period in the late 1980s.

Domaine Leroy, also in the Côte d'Or, has farmed biodynamically since Lalou Bize-Leroy's acquisition of the domaine in 1988. The wines — Chambolle-Musigny, Vosne-Romanée, Gevrey-Chambertin, and several Grand Crus — command among the highest prices of any Burgundy, partly because of their concentration and partly because of extremely low yields that biodynamic management tends to produce. Musigny 1990 and Chambertin 1990 are considered two of the greatest red Burgundies of the modern era.

Coulée de Serrant in the Savennières appellation of the Loire — a 7-hectare monopole classified as a single AOC — is arguably the most famous biodynamic estate in France. Nicolas Joly, who converted in the early 1980s and has become the most public intellectual advocate for biodynamic wine, produces a bone-dry Chenin Blanc of extreme mineral concentration and longevity. Joly's book Wine from Sky to Earth remains the standard text for biodynamic wine philosophy.

## Jules Chauvet and the Birth of Natural Wine

The natural wine movement traces its intellectual lineage largely to Jules Chauvet, a Beaujolais négociant, chemist, and taster whose writings and experiments in the 1970s and 1980s explored the possibility of fermentation with wild yeasts and without the addition of sulphur dioxide. Chauvet's rigorous biochemical analysis of native fermentation — he demonstrated that healthy grapes in a clean cellar could complete alcoholic fermentation without inoculated yeasts or SO2, producing stable wines — provided a scientific foundation for what had until then been either accident or folk practice. His ideas circulated among a small circle of producers before his death in 1989, but their influence on the generation of winemakers who followed was enormous.

## The Loire's Natural Wine Pioneers

The Loire Valley — particularly Anjou, the Layon, and Touraine — became the heartland of natural wine in France in the 1990s and 2000s. Marc Angeli of Domaine de la Sénéchalière in Anjou is among the most important figures: farming biodynamically since the mid-1990s, making wines from old-vine Chenin Blanc and Grolleau with minimal sulphur and no fining or filtration. His Moulin à Vent red and La Lune Chenin Blanc are foundational natural wine texts, and his influence on younger producers in the region is direct and traceable.

The Beaujolais "gang of four" — Marcel Lapierre, Jean Foillard, Guy Breton, and Jean-Paul Thévenet — were the group of producers who absorbed Chauvet's teachings most directly and applied them in Morgon, Fleurie, and the other Beaujolais Crus in the early 1990s. Marcel Lapierre's Morgon, made from old-vine Gamay with wild-yeast fermentation and minimal SO2, is the canonical natural wine red, and continues under his son Mathieu after Marcel's death in 2010. Jean Foillard's Morgon Côte du Py is the most gastronomically serious expression of the style — a wine that ages gracefully for fifteen or more years.

## Low-Sulphite vs No-Sulphite

Sulphur dioxide (SO2) has been used in winemaking since Roman times. It inhibits oxidation, kills spoilage yeasts and bacteria, and stabilises wine for transport and long ageing. The debate in natural wine circles is not whether SO2 is harmful — the quantities used in wine are minute — but whether its presence masks terroir expression or prevents certain fermentative characters from fully developing. "No-added sulphite" wines contain only the small amount of SO2 produced naturally during fermentation (usually under 10 mg/L). "Low-sulphite" wines typically add a small dose at bottling — 20 to 50 mg/L total — compared to the legal maxima of 150 mg/L (reds) and 200 mg/L (whites) in the European Union. The practical difference for the consumer is that true no-sulphite wines require careful handling, cold-chain storage, and relatively prompt consumption; low-sulphite wines are more robust.

## The Funky/Clean Debate

Natural wine's most persistent controversy concerns the presence of volatile acidity, mousiness (a fault compound detectable retronasal at high levels), and Brett (Brettanomyces) in wines that are celebrated by their advocates as expressions of terroir or natural process. Critics — many of them in the conventional and fine wine worlds — argue that these are cellar faults dressed in philosophical clothing. Advocates respond that the same compounds in small doses are complexity markers. Both positions contain truth. The best natural wine producers — Lapierre, Foillard, Angeli, Clau de Nell's Christine Depuydt, Thierry Puzelat — produce wines that are alive and complex without being marred by detectable fault. The worst exploit the philosophy as a license for sloppy winemaking. Tasting discernment remains the only reliable guide.
