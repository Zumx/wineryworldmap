Biodynamic viticulture is a particular kind of organic agriculture
that originated with the lectures Rudolf Steiner gave to a group
of Central European farmers in 1924. It has, over the past four
decades, been taken up by an unusual share of the world's most
admired wine estates — Romanée-Conti, Leflaive, Lalou Bize-Leroy,
Zind-Humbrecht, Beaucastel, Coulée de Serrant, M. Chapoutier, the
Frey family at Trimbach's neighbours, and many more. That uptake
puzzles outsiders. Below is a working explanation.

## What biodynamics is

Biodynamics treats the farm as a single living organism. The
principles are: avoid synthetic chemistry (pesticides, herbicides,
synthetic fertilisers); build soil biology through compost and
cover crops; close nutrient loops on-farm where possible; and use
a set of specific *preparations* — labelled 500 through 508 — to
support fermentation, soil microbial life, and plant health.
Operations follow a lunar and planetary calendar, with planting,
pruning, and harvesting timed to phases that biodynamic farmers
believe affect plant vigour.

## The preparations

Preparation 500 is a horn manure: cow manure packed into a cow
horn, buried in autumn, dug up in spring, and dynamised in water
before being sprayed at very low concentration on the soil. It is
believed to stimulate soil microbial activity. Preparation 501 is
horn silica: ground quartz buried in a horn, similarly applied as
a foliar spray, believed to enhance photosynthesis. Preparations
502 to 507 are compost additives — yarrow, chamomile, nettle, oak
bark, dandelion, valerian — each prepared in specific traditional
ways and added in trace amounts.

## The mystical and the practical

The objection most outsiders raise — that the preparations are
homeopathic-strength and the cosmic calendar is unscientific — is
fair. The honest practical case for biodynamics is not the
mystical one. It is that the system enforces a discipline of soil
care, low intervention, and attentive vineyard work that produces
healthier vines and (often) better wine, regardless of whether
the preparations have measurable direct effects.

## Why so many top estates have adopted it

The list of biodynamic converts among the great estates is long.
The honest answer for why is: the people running these properties
generally already farm organically; they observe, over years, that
biodynamic farming produces healthier soils, lower disease
pressure, more balanced wines, and lower yields; and they accept
the preparations and calendar as part of a coherent system whose
totality they find effective. Some adopt the cosmology
enthusiastically; others treat it as scaffolding for the practical
work. Both routes lead to similar farming.

## The certification systems

Demeter is the main international biodynamic certifier and the
most stringent. Biodyvin (Syndicat International des Vignerons en
Culture Bio-Dynamique) is the wine-specific French certifier. The
Demeter standard requires three years of biodynamic management
before certification and prohibits a broader set of cellar
interventions than organic certification typically does (limited
sulphite levels, no flash pasteurisation, no excessive
filtration). A "biodynamic wine" is therefore both a vineyard and
a cellar claim.

## Biodynamics versus natural wine

The two overlap but are not synonymous. Biodynamics is a vineyard
agricultural method that may be combined with conventional cellar
work or with low-intervention natural-wine cellar practice.
Natural wine is a (loosely defined) movement around the cellar:
spontaneous fermentation, no additives, minimal or zero sulphites.
Many natural wine producers also farm biodynamically, but a
significant share of biodynamic producers (Romanée-Conti is the
classic example) use entirely classical cellar techniques.

## Costs and complexity

Biodynamic farming requires more labour than conventional and
typically more than organic. Yields are usually lower (10-25%
lower than conventional is typical). Compost preparation and
preparation production are time-intensive. The economic case
relies on selling at a price premium — which biodynamic wine
generally commands at the quality end of the market, less so in
the supply-driven mass market.

## What the wine tastes like

There is no single biodynamic flavour profile. The cellar work,
not the farming, drives the taste profile. What biodynamic wines
tend to share is a quality of *energy* or *clarity* — terms
hard to make precise — that experienced tasters report as
distinct from technically similar non-biodynamic wines. Sceptics
note the difficulty of blind-tasting verification. The most
reliable observation is that the longer biodynamic farming has
been in place, the better the soil quality and the more vital the
wine.
