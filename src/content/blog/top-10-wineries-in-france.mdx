France is the reference library of wine. These ten estates are among the most
celebrated on Earth — though the greatest are also the hardest to visit, so
plan accordingly.

## 1. Château Margaux, Bordeaux

A first-growth Médoc estate whose neo-Palladian château is as iconic as its
wine.

## 2. Château Mouton Rothschild, Pauillac

First growth famous for artist-designed labels and a renowned wine museum.

## 3. Domaine de la Romanée-Conti, Burgundy

The most mythologised wine on the planet — a tiny grand-cru monopole;
effectively impossible to visit, a pilgrimage to see the vineyard wall.

## 4. Château d'Yquem, Sauternes

The pinnacle of sweet wine, a hilltop estate above the Garonne.

## 5. Maison Krug / Moët & Chandon, Champagne

Champagne houses in Reims and Épernay with deep chalk cellars and polished
visitor tours.

## 6. Domaine Leflaive, Puligny-Montrachet

The benchmark for white Burgundy and biodynamic viticulture.

## 7. Château Haut-Brion, Pessac

The only non-Médoc first growth, effectively in the Bordeaux suburbs.

## 8. M. Chapoutier, Northern Rhône

A leading Hermitage and Côte-Rôtie house with a strong visitor and tasting
program.

## 9. Château de Beaucastel, Châteauneuf-du-Pape

The southern Rhône reference estate, pioneers of organic Châteauneuf.

## 10. Trimbach, Alsace

A historic family house whose Clos Ste Hune is one of the world's great dry
Rieslings.

## How French winery visits work

France splits sharply: Champagne houses and many Bordeaux châteaux run
professional, bookable tours; the greatest Burgundy domaines and first growths
do not accept casual visitors at all. Realistic access is via the négociant
tasting rooms, village *caveaux*, and the many excellent smaller estates around
the legends. Always book ahead; spontaneous cellar visits are rare in France.

## Plan around the region

Each region is its own trip: Bordeaux's grand châteaux and city, Burgundy's
tiny villages and walled climats, Champagne's two cellar towns, the Rhône's
river route, Alsace's wine road. Pick one, base centrally, and use a wine
guide or driver — appointments and ratings reward local knowledge.

## Taste responsibly

French tastings are serious and frequent — spit, hydrate, eat properly, and
never drive between estates. Regional wine-tour operators solve both logistics
and access to estates that will not take direct bookings.
